# Abstract 

This paper presents ShareVerse, a video generation framework enabling multi-agent shared world modeling, addressing the gap in existing works that lack support for unified shared world construction with multi-agent interaction. ShareVerse leverages the generation capability of large video models and integrates three key innovations: 1) A dataset for large-scale multi-agent interactive world modeling is built on the CARLA simulation platform, featuring diverse scenes, weather condi-tions, and interactive trajectories with paired multi-view videos (front/ rear/ left/ right views per agent) and camera data. 2) We propose a spatial concatenation strategy for four-view videos of independent agents to model a broader environ-ment and to ensure internal multi-view geometric consistency. 3) We integrate cross-agent attention blocks into the pretrained video model, which enable inter-active transmission of spatial-temporal information across agents, guaranteeing shared world consistency in overlapping regions and reasonable generation in non-overlapping regions. ShareVerse, which supports 49-frame large-scale video generation, accurately perceives the position of dynamic agents and achieves consistent shared world modeling. 

# 1 Introduction 

World models extend intelligence from language-centric tasks to visual and spatial reasoning. By modeling 3D environments, they enable agents to simulate future trajectories and interact with the world, demonstrating remarkable reasoning capabilities in game development and embodied intelligence. Recent breakthroughs in video generation models [ 1– 4] have demonstrated their potential to evolve into world models capable of modeling the physical world. Multi-agent shared world modeling represents a cutting-edge direction in the research of world model, serving as the foundational technology for applications such as multiplayer games, multi-robot collaboration, and drone swarms. The aim of a multi-agent video world model is to construct a unified shared world generated by each agent within it with global consistency, while enabling dynamic mutual generation of agents with accurate spatial positioning. Despite significant progress in current world model research[ 5– 13 ], no work has yet focused on building a unified shared world supporting the interaction of multiple independent agents via video generation. Most existing studies are limited to single-view video generation, focusing on temporal geometric consistency; by contrast, works on multi-view generation[ 14 – 16 ] mostly adopt an object-level or small-scale generation paradigm with limited spatio-temporal spans across views, rather than modeling global scenes and multi-agent interactions. To tackle the video generation task in the shared world, we propose ShareVerse , which leverages the inherent context generation capability of large video models[ 3]. To address the mismatch between existing datasets and the requirements of this task, a novel dataset for large-scale multi-agent interactive world modeling is constructed based on the CARLA simulation platform[ 17 ]. covers diverse weather conditions and scenes, incorporates six types of trajectories with inter-agent interactions, and collects massive paired video data and raw camera data of multiple agents under the same spatio-temporal context. Each agent is equipped with cameras of four directions: front, rear, left and right. Camera intrinsics and poses are first preprocessed to raymaps, which are then downsampled and dimensionally transformed to match video features in both pixel space and temporal dimension. Encoded by a raymap encoder, raymap embeddings have the same shape as video features. To fully capture global scene information and generate a shared world, the four-view videos of a certain agent are spatially concatenated, enabling the model to synchronously generate multi-view frames for the agent. This design guarantees geometric consistency across multiple views of a certain agent. For world sharing among multiple agents, cross-agent attention blocks are integrated into the pretrained video model: these blocks concatenate the video features of multiple agents, enabling the interactive transmission of position and frame information across different agents. It thus ensures the consistency of generated frames in spatially overlapping regions and reasonable generation in non-overlapping regions based on historical frame information. Extensive experiments demonstrate that the proposed model can effectively guarantee the consistency of the shared world generated by multiple agents, achieving excellent cross-agent synchronization performance. Moreover, the model can accurately perceive the positions of other agents in videos and generate the corresponding target agents. Ablation studies further validate the effectiveness of the core design modules in this research. The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows: • ShareVerse realizes video generation in the shared world, proposing a novel research task in this field and laying a foundation for subsequent relevant studies. • A data collection pipeline is designed, and a dataset for large-scale multi-agent interactive world modeling is constructed, which contains abundant paired videos with interactive trajectories of two agents. • An innovative model architecture is proposed, which both guarantees the multi-view geo-metric consistency of a certain agent and achieves the consistency of the generated world across multiple agents, while accurately generating dynamic target agents. 

# 2 Related Work 

Video Generation Models .Diffusion models[ 18 – 20 ] have emerged as the dominant state-of-the-art method in video generative modeling. Researchers have adapted diffusion model architectures to the temporal domain, enabling the generation of high-quality video clips containing tens to hundreds of frames [ 1– 4, 21 ]. Furthermore, through techniques such as ControlNet[ 22 ] and Distillation[ 23 , 24 ], related methods have achieved further advancements in generation controllability and efficiency. 

World Models .Driven by action commands, world models aim to predict future states. Studies such as[ 7 , 8, 11 –13 , 25 –27 ] adopt discrete or continuous action signals to enable agents to navigate and interact with virtual environments. To ensure the geometric consistency of generated videos, existing works can be categorized into two types: explicit reconstructions and implicit conditioning.[ 9 , 12 , 28 –30 ] explicitly reconstruct 3D representations and render frames based on these representations as conditions for new video generation. In contrast, [ 6, 11 , 13 ] directly retrieve relevant historical frames as conditions through field-of-view (FOV) overlap, demonstrating efficiency and scalability. However, traditional world models struggle to address shared world modeling, as they fail to guarantee that videos generated by multiple agents depict the same underlying physical world. 

Multi-View Models .Current research in multi-view video generation primarily focuses on object-level or small-scale 4D synthesis. SV4D [ 14 ] generates multiple novel view videos by leveraging both 3D priors in multi-view image generation models and motion priors in video generation mod-els.SynCamMaster [ 15 ] designs a multi-view synchronization module to maintain appearance and geometry consistency across different viewpoints. IC-world [ 16 ] enables parallel and coherent multi-view generation through pixel-wise coupling and instruction-based in-context prompting. These are fundamentally distinct from the work we present, which focuses on multi-agent large-scale world modeling, with the goal of realizing the dynamic interaction and collaborative generation of multi-agents to construct a shared world. 2Figure 1: Illustration of the dataset construction process. # 3 A Multi-Agent Synchronized Video Dataset 

To generate a video world where two agents coexist in the shared world, a video generation model requires paired video data for training. Specifically, the training data must include synchronized visual frames of the two agents captured in the same spatio-temporal context, enabling the model to learn 4D consistent generation. Collecting such data in real-world scenes leads to high cost, and publicly available multi-view synchronized datasets are also constrained by limitations such as small scene scales and high frame overlap rates, which are incompatible with the requirements of this study. Thus, we opt to generate training data via a rendering engine. This approach offers three key advantages: (1) precise camera trajectories can be obtained; (2) perfect temporal synchronization is achievable; and (3) the dataset can be easily scaled up in terms of volume. We have built a complete data rendering and collection pipeline based on the CARLA [ 17 ] simulation platform built with Unreal Engine 4. CARLA incorporates multiple towns, containing diverse topographical elements such as skyscrapers and residential buildings, and supports user-defined agent motion trajectories to drive vehicle movement. The detailed data collection process is designed as follows: first, a large number of main interactive scenes (e.g., straight roads, crossroads and T-junctions) are selected, and the motion trajectories of the two agents are defined. Each agent is equipped with four synchronized cameras (front, rear, left, and right), which capture two sets of multi-view RGB videos (eight video streams in total) synchronously in the same spatio-temporal context. The four viewing angles jointly cover a 360° full field of view, providing comprehensive visual information support for subsequent interactive generation in the shared world. Meanwhile, the intrinsic parameters and poses of the front camera are collected synchronously, serving as the foundational data for subsequent raymap construction. To ensure the validity of visual interaction between the two agents, various typical interactive trajectory modes are designed, including straight meeting and turning meeting, with illustrative trajectory examples shown in Fig. 1. We configure the agents as red Audi TT vehicles. To guarantee the diversity and generalization capability of the dataset, data collection is conducted across three different weather conditions and more than ten differentiated scenes. To enhance the comparability and richness of the data, the start and end points of each basic trajectory are subjected to random translation transformations, repeated three times for each transformed trajectory. The length of a video is approximately 250 frames. To adapt to the input requirements of the video model, the collected video pairs are synchronously clipped into 49-frame segments with an interval of 2 frames (corresponding to 147 frames in the original video) and a cropping stride of 49 frames. This processing method ensures that the motion trajectory of each agent covers a sufficient scale range and effectively preserves the interaction process. Ultimately, this study constructs a large-scale dataset containing 55,000 pairs of videos. 3Figure 2: Method overview. Given one image from each of two agents, where four views are concatenated to depict a scene, ShareVerse performs a prediction task of 49 video frames to generate future videos conditioned on the camera trajectories from users. In the generation process, two agents explore the world, exchange captured visual information, and perceive each other’s positions. 

# 4 Method 

4.1 Preliminaries 

Mainstream video diffusion models [ 18 ] typically involve two processes: a forward (noising) process and a reverse (denoising) process. The forward process can be expressed as: xt = αtx0 + σtϵ where the positive scalar parameters αt and σt determine the signal-to-noise ratio according to a predefined noise schedule, and ϵ is sampled from a standard Gaussian distribution. ShareVerse is based on CogVideoX [ 3 ].CogVideoX consists of a causal 3D VAE [ 31 ] and a Diffusion Transformer (DiT) [ 32 ], where each DiT block is composed of 3D self-attention and feed-forward network(FFN). Given an input video V ∈ R(1+ F )×H×W , the 3D VAE compresses its spatio-temporal dimensions to [1 + F/ 4, H/ 8, W/ 8] , which we denote as [f, h, w ]. DiT then models the latent space distribution. Generation is accomplished by sampling within the latent space and decoding the results back to the pixel space. Building upon this architecture, the proposed model introduces additional interaction modules to enable shared world modeling. 

4.2 Camera Trajectory Processing 

Action signals are needed to enable agents to navigate and interact.We convert camera parameters into raymap embeddings to ensure compatibility with video diffusion models. Formally, given the intrinsic matrix K ∈ Rb×F ×3×3 and camera pose T ∈ Rb×F ×4×4, the conversion pipeline proceeds as follows. 

Mean Normalization 

First, to mitigate extreme values, the translation component t ∈ Rb×3 of the camera pose is normalized by subtracting the common centroid. A random translation is then applied to obtain t′, thereby augmenting trajectory diversity. 

Raymap Construction 

Using the intrinsic matrix K, the camera ray direction rd in homogeneous coordinates is computed for each pixel, with its z-axis component fixed to unity. The rd is then transformed by the rotation component R into the raymap in the world coordinate system. The ray origin ro is set to the transformed translation vector t′ and broadcast across the spatial dimensions. The final raymap r

comprises 6 channels: 3 channels for rd and 3 channels for ro.

Downsampling and Projection 

To align the raymap with the latent feature dimensions output by the VAE, transformations are made 4in both spatial and temporal domains. Spatially, the raymap is downsampled by a factor of 8 using bilinear interpolation. Every 4 consecutive frames are concatenated along the channel dimension. The reshaped tensor is denoted as Ray ∈ Rb×f ×h×w×24 .Every two blocks within the video diffusion model incorporate a raymap encoder Ec, which embeds the 24-dimensional raymap to match the channel dimension of the Diffusion Transformer (DiT). This embedding is then added element-wise to the corresponding features, conditioning video generation on the camera trajectory. 

4.3 Cross-Agent Feature Aggregation 

To ensure the integrity and geometric consistency of the generated shared world, each agent must simultaneously produce four-view video (front, rear, left, right), providing comprehensive visual support for multi-agent interaction. Raw captured videos are first downsampled by a factor of 2, and four views of a certain agent are then spatially concatenated into a single video stream, forming a unified input format. Based on this, a cross-agent attention module is proposed to achieve generation synchronization between two agents. Given the feature representations of two agents Fi ∈ Rb×(f ·h·w)×c (where 

i = 1 , 2), the features are first reshaped to F ′

Rb×(2 f ·h·w)×c, which serve as the input to the cross-agent attention blocks. For the concatenated feature sequence of the two agents, Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE) with double original frames is introduced. The output of the cross-agent attention blocks is projected via a zero-initialized linear layer, then split back into features F ′

The fused features Fi are subsequently processed by the original DiT blocks of CogVideoX. Each cross-agent attention block is deployed immediately after each raymap encoder. Since the raymap embeddings are strictly aligned with the video features Fi in both spatial and temporal dimensions, their element-wise summation enables the cross-agent attention module to effectively exchange positional and visual information between the two agents within the same spatio-temporal context, thereby driving the model to generate a consistent shared world. # 5 Experiments 

5.1 Implementation Details 

We implement ShareVerse based on the CogVideoX-5B-I2V architecture[ 3]. During training, the video length is set to 49 frames with a resolution of 480 ×720. The model is trained for 10K training steps with a learning rate of 2 × 10 −5 and a batch size of 16, all conducted on 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. We initialize the weights of the cross-agent attention blocks using the weights of DiT blocks from CogVideoX, while the raymap encoder and projector are zero-initialized. Since text prompt conditions are not used, an empty text embedding is provided during both training and inference.

5.3 Ablation Study 

To further verify the effectiveness of our method, we conduct ablation studies. We compare training on four-view videos with that on single-view(front) videos, with metrics computed only on front-view 7videos at the same resolution. The results show that the former performs better. This is because the two agents have substantially different perspectives during interaction, while a single view cannot capture comprehensive world information, severely limiting the exchange of visual information. In ablating the way to project camera trajectories, we differentiate between raymap and raw values. The results indicate raymap processing enables agents to better perceive their own and each other’s positions. With respect to model design, the cross-agent attention module unsurprisingly plays an important role in interactive generation. 

# 6 Conclusion 

ShareVerse is a pioneering video world model trained for multi-agent shared world modeling. It provides a large-scale interactive dataset and introduces a novel research paradigm for multi-agent shared world modeling to bridge the gap between existing data resources and task requirements. By spatially concatenating multi-view videos and integrating the cross-agent attention blocks, ShareVerse effectively guarantees multi-view geometric alignment for independent agents and global consistency across agents, laying a solid foundation for embodied multi-agent systems. Future directions could involve extending to more complex multi-agent scenarios with dynamic physical interactions, and building a real-time and long-term consistent shared world model.
