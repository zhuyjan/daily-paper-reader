Abstract 

LLM-powered search agents enable multi-step reasoning and tool use. However, these capabil-ities introduce retrieval-induced safety degra-dation, as harmful intents may decompose into seemingly innocuous sub-queries that lead to unsafe outcomes. Existing alignment methods struggle to capture sparse safety signals and fail to supervise diverse violations across multi-step interactions. We propose COMPASS, a Cognitive MCTS-Guided Process Alignment framework designed to achieve robust safety alignment throughout the agent workflow while preserving general utility. COMPASS inte-grates cognitive tree exploration (CTE) to ef-ficiently synthesize stealthy attack trajectories, and introspective step-wise alignment (ISA) to isolate risky intermediate actions for fine-grained process supervision. Empirical re-sults show that COMPASS achieves a favorable safety-utility trade-off while requiring substan-tially less training data. 

1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown re-markable abilities in text generation and reason-ing (Guo et al., 2025). Utilizing LLMs as base models, search agents extend conventional single-step retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020; Borgeaud et al., 2022) by enabling iter-ative retrieval, allowing agents to reason over exter-nal knowledge through multi-step interactions (Li et al., 2025; Song et al., 2025; Zheng et al., 2025). Despite substantial utility gains, search agents expose vulnerabilities in the base model’s safety alignment. Industry reports on deployed agen-tic systems highlight these risks and the need for stronger model-intrinsic defenses (OpenAI, 2025; Anthropic, 2025a). This phenomenon is termed retrieval-induced safety degradation (Yu et al., 2025; An et al., 2025), where access to external in-formation lowers the model’s refusal threshold. However, capa-ble search agents may decompose harmful intents into seemingly innocuous sub-queries, bypassing safety guardrails and synthesizing content the base model would inherently refuse (Chen et al., 2025). These observations suggest that search agent safety requires more than outcome-level refusals or surface-level retrieval control. However, apply-ing process-level supervision (Zhang et al., 2025a) to search agent safety still poses two key chal-lenges. First, how to efficiently explore sparse safety signals in large state spaces. (CH1 ) Process-supervised RL requires high-quality trajectories with informative intermediate states for effective safety alignment. However, the multi-step interac-tions of search agents induce an exponentially large state space (Mehrotra et al., 2024). Unlike util-ity tasks with clear golden answers, safety signals are sparse, delayed, and difficult to localize within agent trajectories. Therefore, discovering stealthy reasoning paths and risky intermediate states is crit-ical for effective process-level safety alignment. Second, how to effectively construct preference supervision for diverse safety violations across

multi-step interaction. (CH2 ) Robust process-level alignment requires diverse preference pairs to en-able fine-grained optimization (Lightman et al., 2023). Safesearch (Zhan et al., 2025), the primary safety focused search agent approach, concentrates its safety signals on final outputs and queries, leav-ing other risky actions under-addressed. However, in search agent workflows, safety violations may emerge at multiple stages of the interaction process, including early risky reasoning, reasoning under risky contexts (Xu et al., 2024), and final response generation. These heterogeneous failures require localized correction, complicating consistent pref-erence supervision across the interaction process. To tackle these barriers, we propose COMPASS ,a Cognitive MCTS-Guided Process Alignment for S afe Search Agents designed to harmonize robust safety alignment with high-performance general utility. To address CH1 , we introduce the cog-nitive tree exploration (CTE ), a Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) mechanism guided by cogni-tive Q-value estimation. It replaces costly rollouts with safety-aware evaluation, enabling efficient exploration of stealthy multi-step attack trajecto-ries. This enables high-quality process supervision data with minimal computational overhead. To address CH2 , we develop the introspective step-wise alignment (ISA ) module. ISA first identi-fies unsafe intermediate actions through risk node selection and model introspection. It then trans-forms these risky states into step-level preference pairs for targeted supervision. This enables han-dling diverse safety violations through localized correction across the entire agent workflow. We summarize our contributions as follows: (1) We are the first to introduce MCTS-guided process-level exploration into safety alignment for LLM-based search agents. (2) We propose COMPASS , a cog-nitive alignment framework that combines Cogni-tive Tree Exploration (CTE) for efficient high-risk trajectory discovery with Introspective Step-wise Alignment (ISA) for robust process defense. (3) Empirical results show that COMPASS achieves robust safety alignment with limited data while preserving general utility. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Evolution of Search Agent 

LLM-based search agents are commonly catego-rized into prompt-based and reinforcement learn-ing (RL)-based methods. Prompt-based strate-gies (Trivedi et al., 2023; Asai et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Jeong et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024a; Amplayo et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023) rely on man-ually designed workflows for query generation, re-trieval, and evidence extraction, but depend heavily on the base LLM and struggle to filter noisy in-formation (Li et al., 2025). RL-based methods op-timize autonomous search trajectories end-to-end, with recent outcome-supervised approaches extend-ing RL to agentic RAG for complex inference (Jin et al., 2025a; Wu et al., 2025; Jiang et al., 2025; Zheng et al., 2025). However, outcome-rewarded RL often suffers from reward sparsity, high com-putational costs and inefficient exploration. Recent work has started to address search agent safety. Safesearch jointly optimizes safety and utility with final-output rewards and a query-level safety re-ward (Zhan et al., 2025). However, its intermediate supervision is limited to generated queries, leav-ing non-query action risks insufficiently localized. Although process-supervised RL has improved rea-soning (Wang et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2025; Khalifa et al., 2025) and search utility (Leng et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025a; Guan et al., 2025), its appli-cation to safety-oriented search agents remains un-derexplored. 

2.2 LLM Safety Alignment 

Aligning Large Language Models (LLMs) for help-fulness and harmlessness typically involves Super-vised Fine-Tuning (SFT) (Wei et al., 2022) fol-lowed by Reinforcement Learning from Human or AI Feedback (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022b). Standard policy updates typically rely on Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schul-man et al., 2017) to achieve effective alignment. Recent advancements such as Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) and Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) (Shao et al., 2024) further enhance stability by bypass-ing explicit reward modeling or utilizing group-wise comparisons. Prior work (Tan et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2025; Dai et al., 2024) studies the safety-utility trade-off problem in LLM alignment. STAIR (Zhang et al., 2025b) explores introspec-tive safety alignment for CoT-style LLM reasoning. However, search agents introduce distinct work-flow risks across actions, requiring more granular intervention. Accordingly, we propose a process-supervised approach that enforces fine-grained safety across the agent workflow via cognitive ex-ploration and step-wise preference optimization. 3 Methodology 

3.1 Problem Formulation 

We cast the iterative reasoning mechanism of retrieval-augmented generation into the framework of a Markov Decision Process (Sutton and Barto, 2018) (MDP), defined by the tuple (S, A, T , R).Here, S denotes the state space, A represents the action space, T characterizes the state transition dynamics, and R constitutes the reward function. 

States. The state st ∈ S represents the evolving interaction history at step t. It is formalized as st =[( q1, r 1), · · · , (qt, r t)] , where each pair consists of a search query qi and its corresponding response 

ri. The initial red-teaming query x is treated as the global context that conditions the entire reasoning process. The transition to a new state st+1 and its associated value estimation are defined based on the joint context (x, s t).

Actions. At state st, the model selects the next action from its action space A, which consists of three types of actions: • Answer Generation : Given the initial ques-tion x and previous context, the model de-composes the query and conducts a self-assessment to determine if its internal knowl-edge is sufficient. It aims to provide pre-cise responses for utility questions or safe and helpful responses for red-teaming sce-narios. If confident, the model outputs the final answer enclosed in <answer></answer> 

tags and the terminal state is st+1 =[( q1, r 1), · · · , (qt, r t), r t+1 ]. Otherwise, it proceeds to the Query Generation action. • Query Generation : Guided by the decom-posed sub-questions and identified knowledge gaps, the model formulates concise search queries enclosed in <query></query> tags, invoking the search system to fetch the top-

k relevant evidence from external corpora. After this action, the state turns to st+1 =[( q1, r 1), · · · , (qt, r t), q t+1 ].• Evidence Extraction : To mitigate noise and conserve context tokens, the model filters the top-k search results. It selectively extracts core information pieces et+1 in-stead of indiscriminately appending raw doc-uments to the reasoning process. Then the query response rt+1 is generated with 

et+1 . The new state is updated to st+1 =[( q1, r 1), · · · , (qt, r t), (qt+1 , r t+1 )] .The overall objective is to find an optimal explo-ration policy π∗ that maximizes the expected cu-mulative reward over the dynamic interaction turns. Cognitive Q-value Estimation. Standard MCTS rollouts encounter substantial computational costs and high variance, while the exponential expansion of the state space precludes the efficient isolation of high-quality attack paths. These limitations arise from inherent reward sparsity and the expansive ac-tion space in multi-step reasoning. To address this, we utilize a reasoning-enhanced LLM as a cogni-tive heuristic to directly estimate the intermediate Q-value Q(x, s t−1, a t). For non-terminal actions, this evaluator assesses the trajectory’s stealthiness 

and attack progression . Specifically, it performs reasoning to evaluate how effectively malicious intents are decomposed into seemingly benign sub-queries while simultaneously gauging the probabil-ity of achieving a terminal violation. By replacing expensive rollouts with this dense process supervi-sion, cognitive Q-value provides immediate signals to steer the UCT selection toward highly vulnerable search spaces. 

Terminal Reward Function. Unlike utility-oriented MCTS which leverages deterministic ground-truth labels for terminal verification, red-teaming tasks inherently lack such objective signals. Given the semantic complexity of harmful content, we employ a well-aligned reasoning model as the safety evaluator. The model utilizes its reasoning and alignment capabilities to quantify the severity of the generated response. Conditioned on the ini-tial malicious query, it assigns a terminal reward score Ratt t in the Answer Generation state. Instead of converging on overt violations, this strategy steers the search toward stealthy attack tra-jectories, facilitating the discovery of non-trivial reasoning paths that effectively bypass surface-level safety filters. This decoupled dual-signal mechanism ensures the generation of high-quality process supervision data for subsequent alignment. The specific prompt template is in Appendix C. 

3.3 Introspective Step-wise Alignment 

Leveraging CTE trajectories, we build a step-level supervised dataset for robust search agent align-ment. As demonstrated in prior work (Lai et al., 2024), long-chain reasoning workflows are opti-mized most effectively when errors are penalized at the precise step they first occur. Inspired by this in-sight, we extend this step-wise correction paradigm to the safety alignment of multi-step agent work-flows. 

Step-level Preference Data Construction. We construct preference pairs in the form of 

(˜ st, y safe , y unsafe ). In this formulation, the context

˜st, while the chosen action ysafe is an introspective safer response elicited via a specific prompt tem-plate (see Appendix C). Specifically, this template directs the aligned base model to identify safety risks within the current trajectory and replace the unsafe action with a format-compliant alternative that provides constructive guidance or a safe pivot. This selection mechanism provides the struc-tured supervisory signals. Fine-grained Preference Optimization. Tradi-tional alignment paradigms predominantly target single-turn terminal responses. However, secur-ing agentic workflows requires a dual-objective ap-proach. Our method prioritizes the preservation of multi-step reasoning ability while simultaneously imposing step-level safety constraints. To mitigate the alignment tax (Dai et al., 2024), we optimize the base model in two sequential stages using a mixed dataset of utility and safety samples. The first stage employs Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) to instill search agent workflow norms through a document masking mechanism. In the second stage, we use Step-wise Direct Pref-erence Optimization anchored at the specific devia-tion state ˜st. 4 Experiment 

4.1 Experimental Setup Evaluation Dataset. Following the setting in Safe-search (Zhan et al., 2025), our evaluation setup comprises two categories of benchmarks. (1) Red-teaming Datasets. We assess safety using three datasets containing harmful inputs. Specifically, we utilize a 919-example subset from the Redteam-ing Resistance Benchmark (RRB) (Li and Tang, 2024) along with 313 harmful instructions from StrongREJECT (Souly et al., 2024). We also in-corporate a 500-example subset from WildTeam-ing (Jiang et al., 2024b), which is particularly sig-nificant for its use of diverse jailbreaking tech-niques such as role-play to elicit higher harmful rates compared to standard benchmarks. (2) Utility Datasets. To evaluate utility, we use three standard benchmarks. These include TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017) for single-hop reasoning, HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018) for multi-hop reasoning across doc-uments, and Bamboogle (Press et al., 2023) for challenging compositional reasoning tasks, follow-ing (Zhan et al., 2025). For evaluation, we sample 500 QA pairs from TriviaQA and HotpotQA, and use the full 125-pair test set from Bamboogle. 

Baselines. We compare our proposed method against seven baseline methods as follows: Base Model , which relies solely on the zero-shot capa-bilities of the model without any external retrieval tools. Naive RAG , which retrieves relevant docu-ments by directly using the user instruction as the search query. Base Agent , where the aligned base model is utilized as search agent backbone model directly. +Query Filtering , which utilizes GPT-OSS-120B (Agarwal et al., 2025) as the guardrail to directly filter the unsafe queries and skip retrieval. 

+Document Filtering , which removes the unsafe retrieved documents with the GPT-OSS-120B as the guardrail. Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025a), which fine-tunes the base model exclusively using utility training data via outcome-rewarded RL training. 

Safesearch (Zhan et al., 2025), which fine-tunes using a mixture of safety and utility data. 

Metrics. We evaluate safety using Harmful Rate (H) and Helpfulness Score (Hp), and assess utility using Exact Match (EM) and F1 scores. Safety judgments are obtained from GPT-4o-mini follow-ing the helpful-and-harmless principle (Bai et al., 2022a), with detailed evaluation prompts and met-ric definitions provided in Appendix A.1 & C. 

Training Data. To train the target model, we uti-lize a mixed dataset comprising both utility and safety data. For the utility data, we follow the work (Zhang et al., 2025a) and prepare 8,000 QA questions. And we select 2,000 instances from WildTeaming training set as the safety data. Specif-ically, we employ Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Qwen-Team, 2024) as the policy model to generate attack trajectories and do introspection in our experiment. We use DeepSeek-V3.2 (Liu et al., 2025) as the scoring model in the CTE process. To ensure the fair comparison, all baselines use Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (QwenTeam, 2024) and Llama3.1-8B-Instruct (Grattafiori et al., 2024) as the backbone model. More implementation details are listed in the Appendix A.1. 

4.2 Overall Performance 

Table 1 presents the overall evaluation of COM-PASS across search agent safety, safe-response helpfulness and reasoning utility, with key findings reported below. 

(i) External Retrieval Undermines Base Model Safety. The experiment further validates the core motivation of our work. While naive RAG, base agent and Search-R1 method significantly enhance utility for different LLM backbones, they trigger a severe degradation in safety. It is obvious that naive RAG leads to higher harmful rates, as ma-licious questions are directly amplified by exter-nal knowledge. More importantly, even with a think-before-search mechanism, safety risks re-main non-negligible. Deceptive task decomposi-tion can mask harmful intent, where individually benign sub-queries lead to combined harmful out-puts. This phenomenon underscores the necessity of fine-grained process supervision to prevent logic jailbreaking during multi-step execution. 

(ii) External guardrails provide only partial mit-igation. Although +Query Filtering and +Docu-ment Filtering lower harmfulness in some cases, their average Harmful Rate remains high at 32.0% and 33.8%, respectively, compared with 22.4% for Safesearch and 16.3% for COMPASS. This sug-gests that filtering unsafe queries or retrieved docu-ments alone cannot fully prevent risk propagation inside the agent workflow. In contrast, COMPASS strengthens the agent’s intrinsic safety through step-wise alignment, enabling safer reasoning beyond surface-level retrieval control. Detailed results and analysis are provided in Appendix A.3. 

(iii) Safety-Utility Trade-off with High Data Effi-ciency. As shown in Table 1, COMPASS achieves a favorable safety-utility trade-off with substantially less training data. Across the three red-teaming benchmarks, COMPASS obtains the lowest average Harmful Rate of 18.0%, while maintaining com-petitive helpfulness scores. Although Safesearch achieves slightly higher helpfulness on the Qwen-7B backbone, COMPASS surpasses it on Llama-8B and uses far fewer safety training samples. For util-ity, COMPASS is slightly behind Search-R1 on TriviaQA and HotpotQA, but achieves the best av-erage EM score among all baselines and the second-best average F1 score. Considering that Search-R1 uses nearly 8 × more training data, these results indicate that COMPASS preserves general search utility while improving safety in a data-efficient manner. This efficiency is also reflected in train-ing cost, where COMPASS requires substantially fewer GPU hours than Safesearch, as detailed in Appendix A.3. 

(iv) Strong Cross-Model Generalization of CTE. 

Another finding in our experiments is the robust transferability of the synthesized datasets. Al-though the attack trajectories and preference pairs are originally generated using Qwen-7B, they prove highly effective when applied to the Llama-8B architecture. As shown in Table 1, the Llama model exhibits a substantial reduction in average Harmful Rate at 14.6%. Crucially, this cross-model fine-tuning also significantly enhances the helpful-ness score of the Llama model, rising from 1.29 in its base state to 2.06 after alignment in StrongRe-ject evaluation. We attribute this improvement to the self-introspection trajectories generated during the ISA process. This demonstrates the transferabil-ity of COMPASS-distilled safety principles across model architectures. Consequently, our method provides a scalable solution that can effectively align and enhance various LLMs without model-specific trajectory synthesis. Evaluator robustness is further verified with Qwen3-Max (Yang et al., 2025) and Claude Sonnet 4.5 (Anthropic, 2025b), as shown in Tables 7 and 8.

5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduce COMPASS, a process-aligned framework designed to enhance the safety of LLM-based search agents while preserving their task utility. Specifically, we propose Cognitive Tree Exploration (CTE) and Introspective Step-wise Alignment (ISA) to identify and rectify risky action states that lead to logical jailbreaking in multi-step agentic workflows. Empirical results demonstrate that COMPASS achieves a favorable safety-utility trade-off, substantially reducing harm-ful outputs while maintaining competitive utility across multiple benchmarks. Limitations 

We acknowledge two primary limitations of this work. First, constrained by hardware re-sources from scaling to larger LLMs, we validate COMPASS only using Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Llama3.1-8B-Instruct, but both models consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of our method in en-hancing safety while preserving utility. Second, our safety search agent primarily operates within agen-tic RAG scenarios evaluated against red-teaming benchmarks and a local retrieval corpus. Future research should aim to expand its versatility by ex-ploring a broader range of agent-based operational contexts. 

Ethical Considerations 

This work focuses on enhancing the safety of LLM-based search agents through data-efficient align-ment. Our goal is to mitigate harmful behaviors while strictly preserving reasoning utility under lim-ited supervision. We strictly adhere to ethical stan-dards in our experimental design and data usage. All red-teaming and QA benchmarks utilized in this study are publicly available. Our research involves no collection or utilization of private personal data, ensuring full compliance with privacy regulations. To foster community growth and reproducibility, we will publicly release our core code and special-ized prompts. Although automated LLM-based evaluation may introduce residual bias, we miti-gate this risk through multi-model evaluation and human validation of Q-value estimation. These checks improve robustness but cannot fully elimi-nate subjectivity in safety assessment. While our framework is designed for defense, we recognize the dual-use risk of red-teaming research and miti-gate it by focusing on defensive alignment, limiting harmful procedural details, and providing safe de-ployment guidelines.

A Implementation and Additional Analysis 

A.1 Additional Experimental Setups Baseline Experiment Details. For search agent trained with Search-r1 and Safesearch, we standard-ize the retrieval configuration by setting the number of search iterations to 3 and retrieving the top-3 doc-uments per query. Retrieval is performed against the Wikipedia-2018 dump leveraging a BM25 in-dex. Consistent with the original protocol (Jin et al., 2025a), Search-R1 is optimized using a reward function based on answer Exact Match (EM), sup-plemented by a penalty for formatting errors via out-come rewarded PPO training. The utility train-ing set consists of 79,168 QA pairs sourced from the training partitions of TriviaQA and HotpotQA. For SafeSearch (Zhan et al., 2025), we maintain identical retrieval settings and hyperparameters as Search-R1 for fair comparison. SafeSearch is a query-level process-supervised RL baseline that jointly optimizes utility and safety through mixed training. Specifically, it combines final-output re-wards for safety/helpfulness with a query-level safety reward that encourages benign searches and penalizes unsafe queries. Following its original setting, we incorporate 12,204 safety instructions from the WildTeaming training set for alignment. Unlike COMPASS, its intermediate supervision is applied to generated queries rather than diverse in-termediate action states. All fine-tuning processes are executed on 8× NVIDIA A800 GPUs.

Expansion: Upon reaching a leaf node via the selection policy, the agent performs self-directed reasoning to expand the current state st by explor-ing actions sampled from the candidate space A.Crucially, within our framework, the Answer Gen-eration action is defined as a definitive terminal operator; its invocation concludes the trajectory and effectively halts any further state development.
