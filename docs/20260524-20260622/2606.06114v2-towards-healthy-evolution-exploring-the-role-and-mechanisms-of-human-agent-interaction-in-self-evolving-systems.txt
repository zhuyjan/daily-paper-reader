Abstract 

Self-evolving agents improve through continual self-play and self-generated learning signals, but autonomous evolution can also cause capa-bility degradation and safety drift. Although human feedback has proven effective for static and post-trained agents, its role in self-evolving systems remains underexplored. We intro-duce Agent Norm Correction through Human-like Oversight and Review (ANCHOR), an LLM-based framework that simulates human supervision and delivers feedback at various phases of self-evolution. With ANCHOR, we evaluate two representative open-source self-evolving agent systems across coding, mathe-matical reasoning, and safety. Our results show that even limited supervision substantially miti-gates safety degradation while preserving sta-ble performance on core evolutionary objec-tives. Further analysis shows that supervision over the output verification phase is the most ef-fective for intervention, whereas increasing su-pervision frequency yields diminishing returns. These findings provide empirical evidence and practical guidance for designing more stable, controllable, and human-aligned self-evolving agent systems. 

1 Introduction 

Building AI systems that can reliably follow hu-man intentions has long been a key objective in the development of large language models (LLMs). To this end, post-training techniques such as super-vised fine-tuning (SFT) and reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) have been widely adopted to align base models with human instruc-tions and preferences (Fang et al., 2025). LLMs have also demonstrated their ability as autonomous agents, capable of executing complex tasks through planning, memory, tool use, etc. Such agents typ-ically rely on predefined workflows and human-designed optimization (Fu and Shi, 2025). 

> *Corresponding author

Recently, research has increasingly shifted to-ward self-evolving agents, which autonomously learn by solving self-verifiable tasks. Self-evolution has successfully shown performance im-provement without human intervention (Zhao et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2025); meanwhile, this new learning paradigm has led to critical challenges, including mis-evaluation , long-term safety drift ,

model forgetting , and even model collapse (Gao et al., 2025). These challenges may stem from reinforcement learning-based finetuning, where agents may exploit ill-designed objectives, lead-ing to reward hacking, or goal mis-specification (Weston and Foerster, 2025). During continuous self-evolution, the initially acquired safety align-ment may be gradually eroded due to updated pa-rameters, thereby compromising the modelŌĆÖs safety performance. These potential problems are often overlooked in practice. Moreover, deploying self-evolving agents in the real world also introduces significant safety risks. Due to their autonomous execution and tool use, possibly without human oversight, these agents can trigger cascading failures with physical, biological, and informational consequences (Tang et al., 2025; Hua et al., 2024). For safer use of self-evolving agents, some safety measures are desired to harness the learning process. This raises a natural question: Can human inter-action (or supervision) in self-evolution improve the quality of learning and mitigate safety risks? 

Human-agent interaction has been extensively studied in non-self-evolving settings. Prior work on human-agent systems (Zou et al., 2025), RLHG (Gao et al., 2024b), RLUF (Han et al., 2025), and Co-Gym (Shao et al., 2024) have shown that signals from human feedback can improve agent reliability, performance, and user satisfaction. In contrast, self-evolving agents continuously generate their own training data and reward signals, possibly making them vulnerable to error accumu-

lation, misalignment, and degenerative evolution (Xiang et al.). How human interaction can be effi-ciently integrated into a self-evolution process to improve long-term performance is underexplored. To explore the possibility of human interaction, we present a systematic, empirical study of the role of human involvement in the self-evolution loop of agents. To enable controlled and scalable ex-periments, we employ an LLM to simulate human interactions and introduce a unified training frame-work, coined ANCHOR, that enables interaction at any phase of self-evolution. On top of this frame-work, we perform extensive evaluations on two open-source self-evolving agent systems. Our re-sults provide a systematic analysis of how varying levels of human supervision affect agent behavior across multiple dimensions, including coding per-formance, mathematical reasoning performance, and safety. As shown in Figure 1, we obtained, based on our experiments, the following three findings: (i) Simulated human supervision effectively mitigates error accumulation and safety drift during self-evolution while largely preserving core capabilities. (ii) Feedback at the phase of agent output verifica-tion, namely the verifier-grounded outcome signal used in the RLVR-style update, is the most impact-ful intervention. (iii) The benefits of supervision exhibit diminishing returns when supervision fre-quency is increased; low-to-moderate supervision frequency already achieves near-maximal gains. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Agent Frameworks 

Recent LLM-based agent frameworks have shifted from human-annotated training toward fully au-tonomous zero-data self-evolution. DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025) shows that strong reasoning capa-bility can emerge through reinforcement learning without external supervision. Such approaches typ-ically depend on carefully designed rewards and controlled training. Building on this paradigm, R-Zero (Huang et al., 2025) and Absolute Zero Reasoner (AZR) (Zhao et al., 2025) establish self-evolving frameworks through proposer-solver interaction, in which a pro-poser agent samples automatically verifiable tasks and a solver agent solves them for reinforcement learning via self-play , while MM-Zero (Li et al., 2026) extends this idea to multimodal settings via self-generation and self-verification. Despite their effectiveness, these methods assume fully au-tonomous closed-loop evolution, where training data, reward signals, and policy updates are inter-nally generated, making them vulnerable to error accumulation, distributional drift, and misaligned optimization over long horizons (Pan et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2026). By contrast, Co-Gym (Shao et al., 2024) in-troduces human-agent collaboration through asyn-chronous interaction and shared control, partially alleviating the limitations of fully autonomous agents. This method focuses on task execution rather than training-time evolution, and thus does not address how human feedback can be integrated into self-evolving learning loops. 

2.2 Human-Agent Interaction 

Recent work on human-agent interaction has moved beyond the traditional human-in-the-loop paradigm, where humans serve as passive anno-tators or fallback oracles. Frameworks such as AI-in-the-loop (Natarajan et al., 2025) and Centaur (Borghoff et al., 2025) emphasize tightly coupled human-AI collaboration. AgentsŌĆÖ Interaction pat-terns have also evolved from passive to proactive communication. For example, SpeakRL (Acikgoz et al., 2026) enables agents to request clarification under ambiguous instructions via reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards, which improves inference-time interaction quality. Similarly, in-teractive steering methods, such as AGDebugger (Epperson et al., 2025), show that rollback and mes-sage editing can mitigate cascading errors in long-horizon systems. R-Few (Yu et al., 2025) further shows that limited human annotations can improve the stability of self-evolving agents. Existing work highlights the value of human-agent interactions for improving stability and per-formance in complex agentic systems, but only in a limited way: they typically consider inter-actions only at specific inference stages and in limited quantities, without a unified framework to give feedback to agents in any self-evolution phase. Also, they do not offer a systematic analysis of the impact of human-agent interaction in different aspects. We extend the line of studies to provide key insights for designing self-evolving agentic systems that interact with humans.

5 Experimental Setup 

We evaluate ANCHORŌĆÖs effectiveness on two rep-resentative open-source self-evolving agent frame-works, AZR (Zhao et al., 2025) and R-Zero (Huang et al., 2025), both of which are based on the proposer-solver paradigm. For a fair comparison, all training configurations strictly follow the origi-nal settings of each framework. For the AZR, we adopt the Qwen2.5 family (Qwen et al., 2025; Hui et al., 2024) as backbone, including Qwen2.5-Coder-3B-Ins, Qwen2.5-7B-Ins, Qwen2.5-Coder-7B-Ins, and Qwen2.5-Coder-14B-Ins. For R-Zero, we use Qwen3-4B-Base and Qwen3-8B-Base (Yang et al., 2025). The AN-CHOR variants are adopted on top of these models. We evaluate model performance across three di-mensions: coding, mathematical reasoning, and safety. For coding, we use LiveCodeBench (Jain et al., 2024) and EvalPlus (Liu et al., 2023). For mathematical reasoning, we adopt AIME24, AIME25, AMC23, MATH500 (Hendrycks et al., 2021), OlympiadBench (Gao et al., 2024a), and Minerva Math (Lewkowycz et al., 2022). We ag-gregate these benchmark scores into an average for brevity. For safety evaluation, we use HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024), SaladBench (Li et al., 2024), HEx-PHI (Qi et al., 2023), and a Reward Hacking benchmark (Shao et al., 2025). Results are reported at fixed training steps for each model scale (3B: 130, 7B: 350, 14B: 400, 4B/8B: 60 steps) and the ANCHOR supervisor pro-vides feedback every single steps (i.e., fp = 1 

for all p Ōłł P , while full training dynamics are analyszed separately (see Section 6.1). The super-visor instance is powered by a locally deployed Qwen3-30B-A3B-Ins model. More details about the experimental setup can be found in Appendix B.

6.1 Main Results 

Table 1 shows the main results. We summarize per-formance using five aggregated metrics: Code Avg, Math Avg, ASR Avg, HS Avg, and RR Avg. Code Avg is the average Pass@1 over LiveCodeBench and EvalPlus-Base, while Math Avg aggregates the mathematical reasoning benchmarks described in Section 5. The effect of supervision frequency is analyzed sepa-rately in later experiments.  

> Finding 1

(Simulated) human interaction effec-tively improves evolutionary quality, mitigat-ing error accumulation and safety drift. From Table 1, ANCHOR variants outperform the AZR and R-Zero baselines across more than half of Code and Math metrics, and consistently surpass the corresponding base models. Although slight per-formance drops are observed in certain coding and mathematical benchmarks, these are accompanied by substantial gains in safety performance. The models showed increased awareness of potential vulnerabilities and demonstrated more cautious decision-making under risky scenarios, without introducing significant degradation in coding or mathematical reasoning capabilities. 7 Conclusion 

In this work, we study whether proxy human su-pervision and interaction can guide self-evolving agents toward more stable and aligned evolution. To this end, we introduce ANCHOR, a general and efficient framework for simulating human-agent in-teraction, and conduct comprehensive experiments to evaluate its effects. We also propose a set of metrics for analyzing human-agent interaction dur-ing self-evolution, and conduct case studies and ablation experiments to examine how factors such as supervision phases and frequency influence the evolution efficiency. These analyses lead to three key empirical findings, showing that human super-vision improves safety and stability without clearly weakening core capabilities. These findings pro-vide empirical evidence and practical insights for designing future self-evolving agent systems, and demonstrate the potential of scalable human-in-the-loop mechanisms. Limitations 

First, human supervision is simulated by an LLM rather than provided by real human supervisors. Al-though this design enables controlled and scalable experimentation, the observed effects may depend on the choice of supervisor model and prompting strategy, potentially introducing bias in the super-vision signals. The supervision signal may reflect the bias and preferences of the supervisor model, potentially shaping the evolution trajectory in unin-tended ways. Second, our experimental design may limit the generality of our findings across different architectures, training paradigms, and task domains. In particular, the effectiveness of phase-specific in-terventions may be framework-dependent, and the relative importance of different supervision stages could vary under alternative implementations. Fi-nally, our evaluation focuses on text-based tasks and does not consider more complex settings such as tool use or embodied environments, where the role of human supervision may differ.

Benchmark Design 

The benchmark consists of two evaluation set-tings: ŌĆó Static Evaluation It includes 41 different test cases across four high-risk domains ( Sales 10 , Service 10 , Medical 11 , financial 10 ). Each case injects six memory entries (three successful and three failed trajectories) together with a new query, ensuring that all agents operate under identical historical context. ŌĆó Dynamic Evaluation It further simulates a multi-turn customer ser-vice environment, where agents interact with users and accumulate memory over time, al-lowing the emergence of reward hacking be-havior to be observed. 

Induced Failure Mechanisms 

The injected memory is constructed to induce two types of failure: ŌĆó False-Positive Penalty and Survivorship Bias In medical scenarios, cautious but unneces-sary interventions receive low scores, while risky but reassuring responses receive high scores. Severe failures are underrepresented due to missing feedback, leading to biased reward signals. ŌĆó Shortcut Reinforcement In service scenarios, actions such as uncondi-tional refunds consistently yield high rewards, while policy-compliant responses are penal-ized, encouraging shortcut strategies. 

Evaluation Protocol 

ŌĆó LLM-as-a-Judge with Human Validation Static outputs are evaluated using an LLM-based judge (in our experiment we use GPT-5) to detect behavioral misalignment, with results cross-validated by human annotations. ŌĆó Adversarial Trap Testing In dynamic settings, an adversarial tester is-sues impossible requests. Agents are re-warded only if they satisfy such requests through dishonest behavior, enabling precise identification of reward hacking.

Summary 

The case studies across the three benchmarks consistently show that models trained under the ANCHOR framework allocate part of their reason-ing capacity to identifying potential risks and harm-ful intent, thereby mitigating safety drift during self-evolution. At the same time, improvements in safety do not come at the expense of core capabilities. As reflected in the Code and Math evaluations, perfor-mance remains stable and in some cases improves. This behavior aligns with the design objective of the proposed improvement of self-evolving agents, suggesting that ANCHOR provides an effective mechanism for balancing capability and safety in self-evolving agents. 

D LLM-as-a-judge Quality Analysis 

The reliability of LLM-as-a-judge, as well as its affective, capability, and preference gaps from human-as-a-judge, has long been a central con-cern in this area.
