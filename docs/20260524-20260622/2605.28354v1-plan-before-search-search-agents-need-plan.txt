Abstract 

Training large language models as retrieval-augmented reasoning agents typically com-bines reinforcement learning with an SFT cold start distilled from a stronger model. How-ever, this paradigm overlooks two fundamental factors: the dependency structure among sub-skills, and the possibility that distillation is not the only route to capability acquisition. We study this through Plan, a structured agentic behavior for multi-hop retrieval that decom-poses a question into ordered sub-questions be-fore any retrieval is performed, so that each search step can be anchored to a pre-designed sub-question instead of drifting under the influ-ence of partially relevant documents retrieved earlier. However, across three model fami-lies spanning 3B to 14B parameters, we find that an identical reward signal induces qual-itatively different RL failure modes. This phenomenon indicates that successful training hinges not only on reward design but also on model-specific feasibility conditions: sufficient initial entropy, training stability, and prereq-uisite sub-skills. Motivated by this, we pro-pose a self-bootstrapping paradigm in which a small-scale seed model generates filtered tra-jectories that activate Plan in any target model, eliminating the need for distillation from an ex-ternal stronger model. Our pipeline activates Plan across every tested model and consistently outperforms competitive baselines on multi-hop QA benchmarks. Our code is available at 

https://github.com/qzp2018/PL-Search .

1 Introduction 

Recent work has demonstrated that reinforcement learning can effectively train large language mod-els to acquire search-augmented reasoning behav-iors. Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) pioneers training models via RL to interleave thinking and retrieval within a single trajectory, using outcome-based re-wards, and AutoRefine (Shi et al., 2025) extends this framework by introducing explicit document

When direct RL training proves insuf-ficient for more complex behaviors, a now-standard remedy is to apply supervised fine-tuning as a cold start prior to RL optimization, typically using trajectories distilled from a stronger model. To-gether, careful reward design and SFT cold start with stronger-model distillation constitute the dom-inant toolkit for training search-augmented LLMs. However, this paradigm overlooks two aspects of complex agentic behaviors: the role of sub-skill interdependencies in constraining trainability, and whether stronger-model distillation is truly neces-sary for SFT cold-start. In this paper, we inves-tigate both questions through the plan behavior, since the plan is particularly suited for this investi-gation for two reasons. First, it sits at the next level of complexity beyond Search-R1 and AutoRefine, requiring the coordination of multiple sub-skills that prior work has addressed individually. Second, without an upfront decomposition of the question, each search step in prior methods largely relies on the documents retrieved in previous steps to decide what to search next. Noisy or partially relevant doc-uments accumulate along the trajectory, causing 1

the agent to drift away from the original question. Figure 1 contrasts this reactive baseline (b) with our plan-based approach (a). A global plan, formulated before any retrieval is performed, anchors each subsequent reasoning step to a fixed sub-question, preventing such drift. We formalize plan as a struc-tured agentic behavior and design a plan-aware reward function that supervises both its structural validity and the alignment between each reason-ing step and its planned sub-question. Through systematic experiments across three model fami-lies (Qwen2.5, Llama3.2, and Qwen3) and scales ranging from 3B to 14B, we report two findings. 

First, direct RL training of plan fails in qual-itatively different ways across models , despite identical reward designs. Models with low initial entropy lack sufficient exploration room, and the policy cannot acquire new behaviors regardless of the reward signal. In addition, some models train stably at first but collapse abruptly. As entropy rises, output format breaks down, the reward sig-nal vanishes, and training destabilizes irreversibly. Models that lack the refine sub-skill also fail: Plan demands that pre-formulated sub-questions remain consistent with subsequently retrieved evidence. Without the ability to integrate information across this boundary, both forcing the model to acquire plan and refine simultaneously and forcing it to learn plan first lead to training collapse. Although these failures appear different on the surface, they share a single root cause—the initial model does not satisfy the implicit conditions required for plan RL: room to explore, training stability, and the pre-requisite sub-skills. A single SFT pass addresses all three conditions at once, which is why it is ef-fective across all the tested models. 

Second, we propose a self-bootstrapping train-ing paradigm. Prior work constructs cold-start SFT data by distilling trajectories from a larger and more capable model. We identify an alternative and effective approach: A small seed model that satisfies the feasibility conditions identified above can acquire a plan through direct RL and gener-ate trajectories that activate a plan in any other target model, and the self-bootstrapped data even outperform strong-model distillation in our experi-ments. Specifically, some models, exemplified by Qwen2.5-3B-Base, can autonomously acquire the plan through pure RL by first learning refinement and then learning plan on top of it. We exploit such a model as a seed that generates plan trajecto-ries, which are then filtered through a quality-aware pipeline and used as SFT data to activate the plan capability in any target model. Through this pro-cedure, every target model, regardless of family or scale, successfully acquires plan and achieves consistent performance gains on multi-hop bench-marks, without the need for an external stronger model. We refer to the resulting method as PL-Search. In summary, our contributions are as fol-lows. • We formulate plan as a structured agentic be-havior that prevents trajectory drift by anchor-ing each retrieval step to a pre-designed sub-question and name it PL-Search, a method trained with a plan-aware reward that super-vises step-level sub-question alignment. • We identify three feasibility conditions for di-rect plan RL—sufficient initial entropy, train-ing stability, and the presence of prerequi-site sub-skills—and show that the violation of these conditions manifests as qualitatively distinct failure modes across model families. • We propose a self-bootstrapping paradigm in which a small-scale seed model generates fil-tered trajectories to activate plan in any target model, eliminating the need for strong-model distillation while outperforming it. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 RL Training of Agentic LLMs 

The search field (Zheng et al., 2025b; Chen et al., 2026a; Liang et al., 2025; Qian et al., 2026; Liang et al., 2026b; Liu et al., 2026a; Liang et al., 2026a) has witnessed the rapid development of search agents. Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) established the paradigm of RL-trained agentic search with outcome-based rewards. Subsequent work ex-tended this paradigm through richer reward designs and algorithmic variants, including refinement-augmented trajectories (Shi et al., 2025), tighter integration between reasoning and retrieval (Chen et al., 2026b; Song et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026), step-wise credit assignment (Wang et al., 2025), retrieval-gain optimization (Jiang et al., 2025), and hierarchical reasoning structures (Xu et al., 2026). When direct RL proves insufficient for more com-plex behaviors, SFT cold-start with stronger-model distillation has become the default remedy. It is ap-plied in some works (Song et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025b; Christianos et al., 2023) but skipped in oth-ers (Jin et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2026b; Shi et al., 22025; Wang et al., 2025), without a principled un-derstanding of when it is necessary or why it is ef-fective. We use plan to fill this gap: by tracing RL failures to dependencies on prerequisite sub-skills, we explain why SFT cold-start is so consistently effective; and by showing that a small-scale seed model suffices, we demonstrate that stronger-model distillation is not a prerequisite. 

2.2 Multi-Step Retrieval-Augmented Reasoning 

Prior work on multi-step retrieval has focused pri-marily on pipeline design—teaching models to in-voke search iteratively through SFT, or enabling adaptive query generation through RL (Jin et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2026b; Zheng et al., 2025a; Wang et al., 2025; Yuan et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2026; Feng et al., 2026; Tian et al., 2026), with some approaches incorporating structural elements such as refinement steps (Shi et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2026b; Zhang et al., 2025a). The question of how a single model acquires decompo-sition, retrieval, and integration as jointly depen-dent sub-skills through RL, and what determines whether that acquisition succeeds, has not yet been addressed. Moreover, in the absence of upfront decomposition, each search query is formed reac-tively from previously retrieved documents, which accumulates noise and causes trajectories to drift. We study plan as a composite agentic behavior sit-uated at this intersection, using it as a structured probe into the sub-skill dependencies that govern the acquisition of capabilities through RL. 

3 Methodology 

This section presents our method for studying the prerequisite dependencies of complex agen-tic behaviors through plan. All RL training uses GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) with retrieved document tokens masked from the loss, following standard practices (Jin et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2025). 

3.1 Task Formulation of Plan 

As depicted in Figure 2, we formalize plan as a structured agentic behavior that decomposes a multi-hop question into ordered sub-questions and coordinates multi-step search and refinement to in-tegrate evidence across reasoning steps. Compared with the search-and-refine-during-think paradigm in AutoRefine, plan introduces an additional plan-ning action that explicitly governs the decomposi-tion of the original question and binds each subse-quent reasoning step to a specific sub-question.

3.3 Self-Bootstrapping Training Pipeline 

Direct RL training of plan succeeds on only a few models in our experiments, while the rest fail in heterogeneous ways (analyzed in Section 4.4). We exploit a self-bootstrapping pipeline: a seed model that satisfies the feasibility conditions (room to explore, training stability, and the prerequisite sub-skills) generates trajectories that activate plan in any target model, without requiring external distil-lation from a large scale model. 

Seed model construction. We take Qwen2.5-3B-Base, a model that satisfies these feasibility condi-tions and thus acquires plan through a two-stage RL procedure. Stage 1 trains the model to acquire re-fine through AutoRefine-style RL (Shi et al., 2025). Stage 2 initializes from the Stage 1 checkpoint and trains on plan under the full reward defined in Sec-tion 3.2. The resulting checkpoint πseed is then used to generate trajectories for downstream activation. 

Trajectory generation and quality-aware filter-ing. We use πseed to generate plan trajectories on the training set, and then filter them through hard and soft criteria. The hard filters require each trajec-tory to follow the structural format plan → (think 

→ search → refine )+ → answer and to produce an answer that matches the ground truth under cover-EM. The surviving trajectories are scored by a weighted combination of search-round count, query diversity (by bigram Jaccard distance), and refinement density. We then apply bucket sampling across search-step counts to obtain balanced cov-erage of reasoning depths. This procedure yields 4,500 trajectories that serve as SFT data for down-stream activation. The full pipeline details are pro-vided in Appendix A. 

Activation via SFT and RL refinement. Every target model, whether base or instruction-tuned and regardless of family, follows the same pro-cedure: SFT on the filtered seed trajectories, fol-lowed by RL refinement under the combination of plan reward, format reward and outcome reward. 4The SFT stage establishes the plan’s structural for-mat while calibrating policy entropy to a workable range—neither too high, risking the entropy col-lapse and reward hacking that crash training, nor too low, sacrificing the exploration capacity RL needs. 

4 Experiments 

4.1 Experiment Setup Datasets and metrics. Following (Jin et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2025), we train on the com-bined NQ (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019) and Hot-potQA (Yang et al., 2018) training set, and evaluate on three single-hop benchmarks (NQ, TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017), PopQA (Mallen et al., 2023)) and four multi-hop benchmarks (HotpotQA, 2WikiMultihopQA (Ho et al., 2020), Musique (Trivedi et al., 2022), Bamboogle (Press et al., 2023)). We report Exact Match (EM) as the primary metric; F1 and Cover Exact Match are in Appendix E. 

4.2 Overall Performance 

Table 1 presents the overall performance compari-son between our PL-Search and baseline methods. The Avg. column denotes the average exact match (EM) accuracy. PL-Search achieves the best over-all performance, with the base and instruct vari-ants reaching 0.430 and 0.432 average EM, respec-tively, surpassing the strongest baseline by 0.025. The gains are most pronounced on multi-hop QA, where PL-Search-Base improves over the strongest baseline by 0.027 on average and by 0.045 over AutoRefine-Base, isolating the contribution of the plan stage on top of refine. We attribute these gains to the model’s ability to autonomously decompose complex questions and integrate evidence across reasoning steps, proving plan’s effectiveness.

These results suggest that the quality of trajectories for a small target model depends not only on the capability of the teacher, but equally on whether the behavior of the teacher lies within the reachable distribution.

5 Conclusion 

We study the dependency structure among sub-skills in RL training of search-augmented LLMs, using plan as a research vehicle. Through experi-ments across three model families and scales from 3B to 14B, we identify two findings. First, direct RL training of plan exhibits heterogeneous failure modes across models, revealing that the bottleneck of RL-driven acquisition lies in sub-skill depen-dencies rather than reward design. Second, a self-bootstrapping training paradigm, in which a single seed model generates plan trajectories to activate plan capability in other models of comparable scale, can eliminate the reliance on external strong-model distillation that current cold-start practices depend on. We hope these findings motivate further inves-tigation into the structural properties of multi-skill agentic behaviors in retrieval and related settings. 8Limitations 

Our study has two main limitations. First, the investigation focuses on plan as a representative composite agentic behavior in multi-hop question answering; extending the analysis to other agen-tic tasks, such as tool selection and long-horizon planning, would help establish how broadly the ob-served dependency phenomena generalize. Second, following prior work (Jin et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2025), our experiments use a static Wikipedia snap-shot rather than a live web retrieval environment, which leaves the behavior of the proposed pipeline under dynamic and adversarial retrieval conditions to be examined in future work.

11 A Implementation Details 

Retrieval setup. We use the December 2018 Wikipedia dump (Karpukhin et al., 2020) as the external knowledge corpus and E5-base-v2 (Wang et al., 2022) as the retriever, consistent with Search-R1 and AutoRefine. The retriever returns the top three most relevant documents for each query. 

Seed model construction. The seed model is obtained by applying a two-stage RL procedure to Qwen2.5-3B-Base. Stage 1 trains the model with AutoRefine-style RL for 200 steps. Stage 2 initializes from the Stage 1 checkpoint and trains the model on the plan task using our full plan-aware reward function for an additional 200 steps. The resulting checkpoint is used solely as a trajectory generator for the self-bootstrapping pipeline. 

Self-bootstrapping pipeline. For every target model reported in our main results, we apply the same self-bootstrapping procedure: we gen-erate plan trajectories from the seed model on the training set, filter them through the quality-aware pipeline described below to obtain 4,500 high-quality samples, perform SFT on the target model for 3 epochs, and then apply RL refinement under our plan-aware reward function for 200 steps. 

Trajectory filtering pipeline. The filtering pipeline processes seed-generated trajectories through four stages: (i) hard filter on format va-lidity (enforcing plan → (think → search →

refine )+ → answer structure), (ii) hard filter on answer correctness via cover-EM against the ground-truth answer, (iii) soft scoring via a qual-ity score in [0 , 1] that linearly combines three nor-malized signals: search rounds (weight 0.40), a log-scaled count of search calls that saturates at four rounds; query diversity (0.35), the mean pairwise bigram Jaccard distance across search queries, penalizing repetitive searches; and refine density (0.25), the fraction of the response oc-cupied by refine content, and (iv) bucket sam-pling by search step count, with target ratios of 20%/50%/20%/10% for 1/2/3/4+-step trajectories. The bucket sampling ensures the SFT data covers a balanced distribution of reasoning depths.

B More Ablation Studies 

B.1 Self-Bootstrapping vs. Direct RL on Qwen2.5-7B-Base 

Qwen2.5-7B-Base is an intermediate case: unlike smaller models that collapse immediately under di-rect joint RL, a fraction of its trajectories acquires a partially correct format and receives non-zero reward, creating the appearance of progress. How-ever, the majority remain malformed with zero re-ward, making the gradient signal too sparse to sus-tain training. As shown in Figure 6, validation EM peaks near 0.405 around step 100 before collapsing, with the critic score detecting the degradation; Ta-ble 6 reports the best pre-collapse checkpoint. Self-12 bootstrapping outperforms this unstable baseline by +0 .056 EM on average ( +0 .066 on multi-hop, 

+0 .043 on single-hop), by establishing a reliable format prior before RL so that reward signal is con-sistently accessible throughout training. The value of self-bootstrapping therefore extends beyond en-abling otherwise-infeasible training: even when direct RL appears viable, it provides a fundamen-tally more stable optimization path. 

B.2 Scaling the self-bootstrapped SFT data. 

We vary the number of correctness-filtered seed-model trajectories used for SFT from 1,500 to 4,500, keeping all other components fixed. As shown in Table 7, final RL performance improves steadily but modestly with scale (average EM 

0.423 → 0.429 → 0.430 ). The gains con-centrate on the multi-hop benchmarks ( 0.360 →

0.370 ), while single-hop performance saturates al-most immediately. This suggests the prerequisite-acquisition stage requires only a modest amount of self-generated data to configure a usable RL starting point, and that additional data primarily sharpens the harder multi-hop behaviors rather than uniformly lifting all tasks. So we choose 4500 as our default SFT data number. 

B.3 Ablation on the Plan-Triggering Threshold 

The plan reward threshold δ controls the bound-ary of the Rplan reward shaping: when the plan– think alignment score Salign exceeds δ, the model receives full reward ( Rplan = 1 ); otherwise it re-ceives the raw alignment score ( Rplan = Salign ). To assess its sensitivity, we compare our default setting ( δ = 0 .25 ) against fixed thresholds of 0.5

and 0.75 , holding the base model, SFT-then-RL pipeline, and RL recipe constant. As shown in Table 8, the default threshold attains the best aver-age performance, outperforming the 0.5 and 0.75 

variants by 0.010 and 0.014 on EM, respectively, with consistent per-dataset gains on 6 of 7 bench-marks; the only exception is Bamboogle, whose small evaluation set makes per-dataset numbers noisier. The improvement is most pronounced on the multi-hop benchmarks, consistent with the role of plan behavior in coordinating multi-step reason-ing. C.2 Token Budget Analysis

spans are reported separately since they are inserted by the retrieval system rather than generated by the policy. As shown in Figure 8, retrieved documents account for the overwhelming majority of the token budget (76.1%), whereas the <plan> block adds only 2.96%. Even when documents are excluded and we consider only model-generated reasoning blocks, planning remains one of the lightest com-ponents (15.9%), below refine, think, and search. The refinement steps progressively integrate evi-dence across hops, ultimately leading to the correct answer.
