1. Introduction 

Video provides a high-dimensional, continuous, and dynam-ically structured representation of the visual world, making it a natural medium for modeling physical and interactive environments. Recent advances in video generation (Wan et al., 2025; Brooks et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2025b) have demonstrated strong capability in learning complex spa-tiotemporal distributions, shifting research from static con-tent modeling toward dynamic visual modeling. Building 1

on this progress, video world models extend video genera-tion to controllable world simulation (Agarwal et al., 2025), emphasizing human-in-the-loop interaction and world state evolution driven by temporal sequences and agent actions. Existing approaches (Bruce et al., 2024) typically realize interaction through low-level control, e.g. , keyboard/mouse inputs or explicit motion trajectories, which directly specify object movements but offer limited expressiveness for ab-stract, high-level semantic behaviors. In this article, we ex-plore a complementary interaction paradigm that treats lan-guage descriptions as high-level semantic actions, enabling object-centric behavioral control over extended temporal horizons in generated videos. We refer to this paradigm as the Action World Model (ActWM), which semantically extends world models driven by low-level controls. While this formulation can be viewed, at a surface level, as a form of text-and-image-to-video (TI2V) generation, ActWM emphasizes action-conditioned evolution of sub-jects and scenes, rather than the synthesis of globally plau-sible scenes or brief motions from static prompts. Conse-quently, naively reducing ActWM to a standard TI2V task and directly applying TI2V models introduces fundamental challenges. Incomplete Action Execution arises because high-level semantic actions require persistent execution over long horizons, yet existing models often terminate prema-turely (Fig. 1a). Action Hallucination and Weak Seman-tic Grounding occur without explicit planning, resulting in motions that contradict instructions or fail to act on target objects (Fig. 1a). Long-horizon Temporal Incoherence 

stems from the lack of explicit state representation and mem-ory, causing object and scene drift over time (Fig. 1a). More fundamentally, these failures are exacerbated by Open-loop Error Accumulation , as most TI2V methods generate with-out intermediate feedback or correction. To address these challenges, we propose SPIRAL , an agentic, self-improving framework with reflective planning agents for ActWM. SPIRAL replaces open-loop, one-shot generation with a closed-loop think–act–reflect paradigm. Given a high-level semantic action prompt, a VLM-driven 

PlanAgent grounds the action in the visual context and decomposes it into object-centric, step-wise sub-actions. Video generation is reformulated as a multi-step, autore-gressive, chunk-wise process, with iterative refinement guided by a CriticAgent that evaluates action fidelity and visual consistency, while a memory module preserves long-horizon object and scene consistency. Crucially, this closed loop induces stochastic action trajectories and determinis-tic critic rewards, enabling RL optimization and continual 

Progressive-Evolution . Together, SPIRAL achieves precise and sustained grounding of high-level semantic actions into coherent, long-horizon video dynamics. Beyond pipeline design, realizing SPIRAL in practice re-quires adapting general VLMs into action planning and video critic experts, and transforming generic TI2V models into step-wise ActWMs. To this end, we introduce ActWM-Dataset, constructed by re-annotating existing action–video pairs into multi-step procedures, comprising 24,616 tasks with 118,156 step-level annotations. The dataset supports fine-tuning of PlanAgent and adaptation of video world models, while the CriticAgent is trained via supervised dis-tillation from large VLMs followed by reward modeling to provide reliable verification. To evaluate ActWM, we fur-ther introduce ActWM-Bench, a benchmark for assessing long-horizon action grounding and temporal consistency. Extensive experiments demonstrate that SPIRAL can be seamlessly integrated with diverse TI2V models, yielding consistent improvements on ActWM-Bench and VBench, with ablations validating the roles of individual agents and the RL continuous training pipeline. Our key contributions are summarized as follows: • We propose SPIRAL , a closed-loop, agentic framework that extends one-shot open-loop video generation with reflective planning agents for ActWMs. • We introduce a large-scale dataset and benchmark for ActWMs, enabling step-wise training and evaluation. • We further adopt a closed-loop training scheme that com-bines SFT with RLVR, leading to consistent improve-ments across diverse TI2V backbones. 

2. Related Work 

Agents for Video Generation. Recent work has explored incorporating chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning and inter-mediate planning into video generation to improve con-sistency and controllability (Guo et al., 2025b). These approaches typically decompose high-level prompts into structured intermediate signals, and have evolved from pre-planning (Liao et al., 2025a) or post-refinement (Zhuo et al., 2025) toward “thinking while generating” paradigms (Guo et al., 2025a). Representative examples include Edit-Thinker (Li et al., 2025a), which frames image editing as iterative reasoning, and VideoAgent (Soni et al.) and Co-Agent (Zeng et al., 2025b), which cast video generation as a closed-loop, agentic process with planning, synthesis, and verification. In contrast to these works, we explicitly formulate a closed-loop framework with reflective planning agents and an explicit memory module, enabling sustained grounding of high-level actions over long temporal horizons. 

Video World Models. Video world models aim to learn environment dynamics by predicting future observations conditioned on past observations and actions (Ha & Schmid-huber, 2018). Unlike prior test-time or heuristic self-improvement approaches, our method enables closed-loop reinforcement learning with critic-derived signals, allowing policies to progressively evolve through group-relative optimization. 

3. Method 

3.1. Overview Problem Formulation. We formulate long-horizon, action-conditioned video generation as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) defined by the tuple T = ⟨g, S, V⟩ . Given a global goal g, the objective is to synthesize a coherent video trajec-tory V = {v1, . . . , v T } guided by a sequence of structured steps S = {s1, . . . , s T }. At each timestep t, the system jointly generates the plan st and video segment vt condi-tioned on historical context, maximizing cumulative rewards that reflect action completion and temporal coherence. 

Overall Framework. To ensure long-term consistency and controllability, we propose a hierarchical, closed-loop framework (Fig. 2) that operates iteratively : (1) a PlanA-gent (πplan ), serving as the high-level policy, employs CoT reasoning to decompose the global goal g into structured atomic plans st; (2) a World Model (πwm ) functions as the execution policy, synthesizing video segments vt con-ditioned on the plan st and context memory Mt−1; (3) 

a CriticAgent (C) evaluates action-video alignment via a reward rt = C(vt, s t), triggering a feedback loop for refine-ment upon detecting failures; (4) World Memory accumu-lates successful transitions as Mt = Mt−1 ∪ { (st, v t)} to maintain global coherence. Collectively, this cycle instanti-ates a think–act–reflect loop, advancing beyond one-shot generation toward a robust plan-and-verify formulation. 

3.2. PlanAgent: Structured Reasoning and Planning 

Directly conditioning a world model on a high-level goal g

in a one-shot manner often leads to temporal hallucinations or incomplete execution. To mitigate this, we employ a 

PlanAgent (parameterized by a VLM πplan ) to explicitly decompose abstract goals into executable action sequences. Formally, given a goal g and memory context M, the agent employs Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning to synthesize a structured plan sequence: 

S = {s1, . . . , s T }, S ∼ πplan (· | g, M) (1) 

Each atomic plan is defined as a tuple st = ( at, c pre t , c post t ),where at denotes the language action instruction, while cpre t

and cpost t represent physical pre- and post-conditions. For 3SPIRAL: A Closed-Loop Framework for Self-Improving Action World Models via Reflective Planning Agents 

instance, for an action at=‘open the jar’ , the agent specifies 

cpre t =‘jar closed’ and cpost t =‘lid removed’ . Crucially, this CoT paradigm compels the agent to explicitly reason about causal dependencies, ensuring that planned actions are phys-ically feasible rather than merely semantically relevant. The PlanAgent undergoes a hierarchical training process: we first conduct Instruction Tuning (IT) on our curated 

ActWM-Dataset (Sec. 4.1) to instill CoT-based structured planning capabilities, followed by Direct Preference Opti-mization (DPO) to enforce alignment with physical reality and temporal logic. This regimen empowers the agent to not only capture structured causal dependencies but also effec-tively mitigate logical hallucinations in complex tasks. De-tailed implementation details are provided in Appendix A.1. 

3.3. Action-Conditioned World Model 

The World Model serves as an executor that translates each atomic plan st into pixel-level dynamics. We instantiate this component as a video diffusion policy πwm . To ensure long-term consistency, the synthesis of the current video segment vt is conditioned on both the action instruction at

and retrieved historical context from the World Memory:      

> vt∼πwm (· | at,Encoder (Mt−1)) (2)

The instruction at specifies the intended motion or interac-tion, while the historical memory Mt−1, stored as visual key frames or latent KV-caches, provides contextual guid-ance via encoded representations. Generation is performed in a streaming manner, where each segment corresponds to a single planned step, and the World Memory preserves motion continuity across the long-horizon sequence. To bridge the gap between generic short-clip generators and procedural long-horizon action generation, we adopt a 

Streaming Long-Tuning strategy. Crucially, our framework supports the plug-and-play integration of arbitrary world models; by performing Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on 

ActWM-Dataset (Sec. 4.1), we empower generic T2V and I2V backbones to follow step-wise action instructions over extended horizons (see Appendix A.2 for a concrete instan-tiation). To systematically evaluate action controllability under this setting, we further introduce the ActWM-Bench 

(Sec. 4.2), which assesses a range of modern world models within our closed-loop framework. 

3.4. CriticAgent: Reward and Closed-Loop Feedback 

Despite structured planning, open-loop generation remains susceptible to execution failures, such as missed actions, post-condition violations, and physical inconsistencies. To address this, we employ a CriticAgent (parameterized by a VLM C) to enforce closed-loop verification and feedback. For each generated segment vt, the CriticAgent assesses its alignment with the plan st across five key dimensions: ac-tion adherence, object interaction, goal achievement, tempo-ral coherence, and physical realism . It yields a scalar reward 

rt ∈ [0 , 1] quantifying action execution quality, alongside textual feedback ft for diagnosis:    

> rt, f t=C(vt, s t)(3)

Leveraging these signals, we implement a dual-level feed-back mechanism: (1) Inner Loop (Local Refinement): If 

rt falls below a threshold τ , indicating minor artifacts or partial action completion, the feedback ft is used to refine the instruction at (e.g., emphasizing unmet post-conditions) for immediate regeneration via the World Model; (2) Outer Loop (Global Replanning): If generation fails consecu-tively for K attempts, implying an infeasible plan (e.g., missing pre-conditions or incorrect action ordering), the failure propagates to the PlanAgent to trigger the replanning of trajectory S starting from step t.To ensure robust verification, the CriticAgent undergoes a two-stage training regimen: first, we distill judgments from large VLMs (e.g., Gemini-3-Pro) via Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) ; subsequently, we refine the model via Pair-wise Reward Modeling (RM) using a Bradley-Terry objec-tive to enhance discriminative accuracy. Detailed implemen-tation protocols are provided in Appendix A.3.

4. Dataset and Benchmark 

4.1. ActWM-Dataset Construction 

To support framework training, we present ActWM-Dataset ,which provides structured ⟨g, CoT , S, V⟩ supervision. It integrates global reasoning with step-wise action sequences grounded in video segments, providing a robust foundation for long-horizon planning and consistent video generation. 

Data Sourcing and Preprocessing. To ensure a diverse coverage of real-world tasks, we aggregate videos from four datasets: Ego4D (Grauman et al., 2022; Song et al., 2023), Ego-Exo4D (Grauman et al., 2024), COIN (Tang et al., 2019), and EPIC-KITCHENS (Damen et al., 2018). These sources provide a broad range of procedural activities across egocentric and exocentric viewpoints. To remove redundant or irrelevant content, we apply an automated shot detection pipeline. This process filters out low-quality or ambiguous clips, retaining only those segments that strictly align with significant action boundaries for annotation. 

Hierarchical Annotation Pipeline. We develop an auto-mated pipeline to convert raw video clips into structured annotations ⟨g, CoT , S, V⟩ , effectively bridging visual sig-nals with explicit step-wise actions. The process consists of two strategic stages: Stage 1: Step-wise Action An-notation. This stage instantiates the structured step tuple 

st = ( at, c pre t , c post t ) from visual observations. By taking video clips vt as input, we leverage a VLM (e.g., GLM-4.5V (Zeng et al., 2025a)) to identify fine-grained action instructions at (specifying verb-object-tool interactions) and infers physical state transitions (cpre t , c post t ). This yields a sequence S of actions that explicitly models the physical dependencies. Stage 2: CoT Planning Generation. We employ an LLM to orchestrate global reasoning logic based on the goal g and sequence S. By synthesizing a reason-ing narrative (e.g., <think> ... </think> ), the model transforms disjoint steps into a coherent, logically grounded plan, providing intermediate supervision for long-horizon CoT-based planning. 

Quality Verification and Human Alignment. To ensure dataset integrity, we implement a hierarchical verification pipeline that integrates VLM filtering with human evalu-ation. Specifically, Qwen3-VL-235B (Bai et al., 2025) is employed as a verifier to filter samples with suboptimal video–text alignment. Subsequent human verification on a random subset yields a 93% alignment rate, confirming that our annotations faithfully capture both action semantics and causal dependencies. This validation ensures a reliable foundation for training agents and world models. 

Dataset Statistics and Analysis. ActWM-Dataset com-prises 24,616 procedural tasks, yielding 118,156 step-level action-video pairs. As illustrated in Fig. 3, the dataset ex-hibits substantial diversity across three pivotal dimensions: (1) Scenes , spanning a wide range of environments from indoor to outdoor settings; (2) Perspectives , balancing ego-centric and exocentric views to facilitate cross-view gener-alization; and (3) Complexity , covering a broad spectrum of temporal durations and task-step lengths. Its scale and structured nature make ActWM-Dataset uniquely positioned 5SPIRAL: A Closed-Loop Framework for Self-Improving Action World Models via Reflective Planning Agents 

to support the development of planning-oriented agents and action-conditioned video generation. 

4.2. ActWM-Bench Construction 

To systematically evaluate the long-horizon controllability and physical plausibility of world models, we introduce 

ActWM-Bench , a comprehensive evaluation suite featuring hierarchical difficulty levels and multi-dimensional metrics. 

Eval Prompt Suite. We curate 300 evaluation prompts stratified into three difficulty levels to probe temporal gener-alization: Simple (1-3 action steps, < 20 s video duration), 

Medium (3-5 steps, 20-40s), and Hard (> 5 steps, > 40 s). To ensure robust coverage, the suite spans diverse scenes 

(indoor, outdoor), perspectives (egocentric, exocentric), and 

procedural activities (e.g., sports, maintenance, cooking). 

Evaluation Metrics. We adopt a hybrid evaluation pro-tocol. We report Static and Dynamic Quality using VBench (Huang et al., 2024) to assess general visual fi-delity. However, since our task focuses on procedural action-conditioned video generation, such generic metrics are in-sufficient to evaluate whether actions are correctly executed. Thus, our primary evaluation target is Action Quality . To this end, we introduce a multi-agent action evaluator with GPT-5 as the reasoning engine, which performs compre-hensive evaluation via multi-agent collaboration and cross-verified chain-of-query reasoning. The agent evaluates Ac-tion Completeness, Motion Smoothness, Object Interaction, and Physical Fidelity. Details are provided in Appendix B.

Impact Statements 

This paper introduces SPIRAL, a closed-loop think–act–reflect framework for action-conditioned video generation, together with the ActWM-Dataset and ActWM-Bench, with the goal of improving long-horizon action fidelity, temporal consistency, and the evaluation of Action World Models. The primary objective of this work is to advance methodological research in video world models, agentic generation, and reinforcement learning–based optimization, which may support future studies in simulation, embodied AI, and interactive systems. All datasets in this work are constructed by re-annotating existing publicly available datasets. No new raw video content is collected, and no additional personal or sensitive data are introduced beyond those present in the original sources; accordingly, the data construction process follows established practices in prior research on video generation and procedural action understanding. As with prior work on generative video models, the tech-niques studied here could in principle be applied in broader content generation settings. However, this work focuses on action execution, physical fidelity, and benchmark-driven evaluation, and does not introduce capabilities specifically designed for identity manipulation, targeted deception, or surveillance. We therefore believe that the ethical consider-ations and potential societal impacts of this work are con-sistent with those commonly associated with advances in generative video modeling and agent-based learning.

Architecture and Training Configuration. We instantiate the PlanAgent using Qwen3-VL-8B (Bai et al., 2025) as the backbone. To enable structured planning while preserving pre-trained multimodal capabilities, we apply Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) to all linear layers in the attention and feed-forward networks. Training proceeds in two sequential stages: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) .

(1) SFT optimizes the model to internalize Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning and the required strict JSON schema. (2) DPO aligns outputs with physical plausibility and temporal logic by maximizing the margin between valid and flawed plans. Both stages use the AdamW optimizer with a cosine scheduler and a global batch size of 8 (via gradient accumulation). We set the context window to 8,192 tokens to accommodate interleaved visual history and extensive reasoning traces. Table 5 summarizes the hyperparameters. 

DPO Preference Construction. A critical component is constructing high-quality preference pairs (yw, y l) to suppress hallucinations and physical violations. Unlike standard datasets containing only ground truth, we employ a Teacher-Student Distillation pipeline to synthesize these pairs from ActWM-Dataset .

Chosen Response ( yw): We prompt a strong teacher (GPT-5.1) to generate a Chosen response containing a rigorous CoT rationale (in <think > tags) decomposing the goal, followed by a valid JSON plan. The teacher ensures the plan successfully realizes the global goal depicted in the ground-truth video. 12 SPIRAL: A Closed-Loop Framework for Self-Improving Action World Models via Reflective Planning Agents 

Rejected Response ( yl): We prompt the teacher to generate a Hard Negative that starts plausibly but fails to achieve the goal due to injected errors, such as missing pre-conditions (e.g., pouring without opening), temporal disorder, or object hallucinations. This Model-in-the-Loop synthesis ensures rejections are subtle and contextually relevant, providing stronger learning signals than random negative sampling. All generated data is filtered for strict schema adherence before training.

A.2. WorldModel Implementation Details 

Our framework adopts a flexible, model-agnostic paradigm for the Action-Conditioned World Model ( πwm ), designed to support the plug-and-play integration of arbitrary Text-to-Video (T2V) or Image-to-Video (I2V) backbones. The core of our approach lies in utilizing the structured instruction-action pairs from ActWM-Dataset to perform Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), thereby endowing generic video generators with fine-grained, long-horizon instruction-following capabilities. 

Model Instantiation. To demonstrate the effectiveness of our SFT strategy, we present a concrete instantiation using Stable-Video-Infinity (Li et al., 2025b), which leverages the Wan2.1-I2V-14B (Wan et al., 2025) as the foundational backbone. To adapt this model for precise action control while preserving its massive pre-trained generative prior, we apply Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) to the query, key, value, output projection, and feed-forward network layers ( q, k, v, o, f f n )of the DiT blocks. We set the LoRA rank r = 64 and alpha α = 128 . The model processes video data encoded by the Wan2.1 VAE and text instructions via the T5-XXL encoder. Training is conducted using DeepSpeed Stage 2 with BF16 mixed precision to optimize memory efficiency. 

Progressive Resolution Training. Since ActWM-Dataset contains videos with diverse aspect ratios, we adopt a Progressive Resolution Training strategy to achieve high-fidelity generation across different spatial dimensions. We categorize the training data into resolution buckets and fine-tune the model in three sequential stages, progressively increasing spatial complexity while decaying the learning rate: (1) Stage 1 (Base Adaptation): The model is trained on 480 × 480 square clips with a learning rate of 1.0 × 10 −4 for 10 epochs to establish basic instruction alignment; (2) Stage 2 (Rectangular Adaptation): We increase the resolution to 640 × 480 with a reduced learning rate of 6.0 × 10 −5 for 3 epochs; (3) Stage 3 (High-Res Adaptation): The model is fine-tuned on 832 × 480 inputs with a learning rate of 4.0 × 10 −5 for 3 epochs. This curriculum allows the model to adapt to new spatial distributions and instruction complexities without catastrophic forgetting of the base motion dynamics. 14 SPIRAL: A Closed-Loop Framework for Self-Improving Action World Models via Reflective Planning Agents

B. Evaluation Details for Action Quality 

Single-turn, direct QA evaluations often lack reasoning depth and fail to scrutinize fine-grained visual details. To address these limitations, we design a multi-agent evaluation system and adopt the Chain-of-Query mechanism (Han et al., 2025) to capture the nuanced alignment between generated videos and text instructions. Specifically, we employ a four-stage pipeline to assess the action quality between generated videos and text prompts. Initially, the video description agent (VLM) processes video frames alongside action decomposition data (action steps and post-conditions) to generate a chronological description, verifying alignment with action steps and checking post-condition satisfaction. Subsequently, based on video descriptions, two specialized assistant agents (LLMs) raise queries based on distinct evaluation dimensions: one verifies whether all action steps are executed and post-conditions met to identify omissions, while the other scrutinizes object interaction realism, transition smoothness, and physical fidelity. Following this, the answer agent (VLM) reviews the video to address the synthesized chain of queries from both assistant agents; it performs rigorous verification to tabulate the completion status for each action step, analyzes overall flow coherence, and provides detailed answers to the inquiries. Finally, the score agent (VLM) computes four quantitative metrics, i.e., Action Completeness, Object Interaction, Action Smoothness, and Physical Fidelity, to produce a complete report, thereby ensuring comprehensive and accurate evaluation through multi-agent collaboration and cross-verified chain-of-query reasoning. We elaborate on the detailed definition of these quantitative metrics below: 

Action Completeness. Measures the proportion of action steps actually completed in the video. An action step is considered 18 SPIRAL: A Closed-Loop Framework for Self-Improving Action World Models via Reflective Planning Agents 

complete only if it is executed and all post-conditions are met. This metric reflects whether the video fully presents all action steps required by the prompt. 

Motion Smoothness. Evaluates the coherence and fluidity of action sequences, scored on a scale of 1-5. This metric focuses on the quality of transitions between different action steps, the presence of abrupt cuts or jumps, the continuity of motion, and inter-frame consistency. 

Object Interaction. Assesses the correctness and realism of object/tool usage in the video, scored on a scale of 1-5 (or N/A for cases without object interaction). Evaluation criteria include the method of grasping/holding, contact quality, accuracy of usage, and the plausibility of the object’s physical behavior. 

Physical Fidelity. Evaluates whether actions adhere to real-world physical laws and natural motion patterns, scored on a scale of 1-5. It scrutinizes the realism of body mechanics, the accuracy of gravity and physics, the plausibility of object deformations, and the presence of artifacts or clipping.

D. Limitation 

While SPIRAL demonstrates strong performance in long-horizon action-conditioned video generation, it still has several limitations. First, the framework relies on a closed-loop think–act–reflect process with explicit planning and verification, which introduces additional system complexity and may increase inference latency compared to one-shot generation paradigms. A promising direction for future work is to further amortize planning and verification into the world model itself, reducing the need for repeated external reasoning during generation. Second, SPIRAL depends on learned PlanAgent and CriticAgent models to decompose actions and assess execution quality. Although effective in practice, errors or ambiguities in planning or critique may propagate across steps in long-horizon generation, suggesting opportunities to improve robustness through stronger joint training or more compact feedback representations. Third, the current action formulation focuses on sequential, high-level semantic actions and does not explicitly model fine-grained control signals or tightly coupled physical interactions.
