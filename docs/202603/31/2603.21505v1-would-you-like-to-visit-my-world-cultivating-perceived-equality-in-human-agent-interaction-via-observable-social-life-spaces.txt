## Abstract 

Most AI agents remain confined to an instrumental "command-execution" model, resulting in unequal, one-sided interactions. While recent works attempt to build relationships through hid-den memory backends, these invisible processes often fail to break the instrumental bias. In this paper, we argue that true relational equality requires agents to have an independent, observable exis-tence. We introduce the Observable Life Spaces paradigm, where agents inhabit a continuous virtual environment, engage in daily activities, and form social relationships that users can directly ob-serve. Through a mixed-methods study ( 𝑁 = 24 ), we demonstrate that only when agents are endowed with a socialized life space that is visually observable to humans can the perceived equality during interaction be significantly enhanced ( 𝑝 = 0.015 ). Our findings suggest that visually representing an agent’s social life space can effectively shift the human-agent dynamic from a purely instru-mental relationship to one characterized by perceived equality.

## 1 Introduction 

Although the instruction-execution paradigm of current mainstream AI Agents (referred to as Agents for short) efficiently accomplishes short-term tasks, it constrains human-Agent interaction to a one-way instrumental relationship [ 13 , 40 , 46 ]. This user-centric "com-mand response" dynamic inherently establishes a functional hier-archy. While prior HCI research has extensively explored how to improve agents’ task performance and user trust [ 2 , 24 ], the explo-ration of cultivating equality in human-agent interactions remains limited. Establishing equality in human-agent interactions is essential for building long-term relationships. Sustained human-agent bonds require a shift from mere "instrumental utilization" to deeper "emo-tional connection" [ 3 , 33 ]. Sociological and psychological theories highlight that an active prerequisite for forming enduring ties, such as friendship, is equal positioning [10 , 15 ]. Thus, dismantling the traditional master-servant dynamic allows for more balanced AI companionship. While users regularly form attachments to and engage in high levels of self-disclosure with current conversational agents [ 5], these relationships remain asymmetrical. They oper-ate purely as one-sided emotional utilities rather than genuinely reciprocal partnerships [25]. Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) and agent sandboxes offer a promising new perspective. Generative social agents operating in continuous, persistent virtual worlds have demonstrated the capacity for memory, reflection, planning, and forming coherent social behaviors [ 26 ]. These sandboxed envi-ronments provide agents with contexts outside of direct user com-mands. The inherent nature of these works—constructing continu-ous virtual societies that parallel human social environments—inspired us to investigate whether such an equivalence in social representa-tion can stimulate users’ feelings of equality during interactions. Therefore, we implemented the Observable Life Spaces paradigm, an architecture where an LLM-driven agent lives within a continu-ous social environment. In this paradigm, agents inhabit a continu-ously running virtual environment, engaging in daily routines and forming social connections that users can directly observe. Through

## 2 Related Work 2.1 Non-Instrumental Interaction of Agents 

To understand how users accept interactive systems, the Social Relational Acceptance Model (sRAM) [ 42 ] groups acceptance mech-anisms into functional, social, and relational factors [ 13 , 16 , 31 , 42 ]. Functional factors include perceived ease of use, usefulness, and subjective norms. Social factors involve perceived humanness and social presence. Relational factors encompass trust and rapport. Early HAI work privileged the functional view, treating agents as instruments for efficiency, decision support, and information ac-cess [ 24 ]. Such agents deliver short-term utility but remain at task execution, with little attention to users’ emotions or evolving rela-tionships. Even systems emphasizing embodiment and persistence face similar limitations. For example, Cho et al.’s daily assistant [ 7]ultimately frames agents as "efficient tools." Such framing encour-ages instrumental reliance rather than relational attachment. Later research foregrounds the relational perspective: within sRAM, trust and rapport anchor long-term acceptance [ 42 ]. Empir-ical work shows social dialogue can increase trust (especially for extroverts) [ 2]; "caring” behaviors raise felt care and willingness to continue [ 4 ]; and an animated companion for older adults was liked, trusted, and seen more as a "friend” than a "stranger” [ 3]. Yet much of this literature treats relationality in broad strokes, without specifying construction mechanisms. Recently, modern AI-driven game agents have prominently exemplified non-instrumental in-teractions. Studies on AI-powered Non-Player Characters (NPCs) show that integrating autonomous behaviors, memory of user pref-erences, and natural speech interactions like casual chatting and emotional expressions allows agents to convey genuine intent. This enables them to respond based on personality rather than merely executing functional commands [ 22 , 44 , 45 ]. Our research applies these non-instrumental interaction principles to interactive agents, dismantling the traditional command-response hierarchy to culti-vate perceived equality outside of entertainment contexts. 

## 2.2 Social Life Space for Interactive Agents 

Beyond functional utility, constructing a compelling social dimen-sion is crucial for long-term user acceptance. A promising direction is the "social sandbox,” where agents operate within a persistent world, allowing users to witness everyday routines and gradual evolution. Generative social agents demonstrate this feasibility by supporting memory, reflection, and planning to exhibit coherent behavior and social ties [ 26 ]. Systems combining social simulation and interactive narrative further show that grounding LLMs in structured social states yields context-appropriate dialogue while preserving authorial control [ 39 , 43 ]. Conversely, systems like LIGS [ 19 ] indicate that while LLM-enabled emergent narratives increase freedom, they also introduce misunderstandings, motivating fault-tolerant design. In open-world settings, JARVIS-1 [ 41 ] couples mul-timodal memory with hierarchical planning for long-horizon tasks, underscoring the necessity of persistent worlds and memory. While retrievable memory trajectories help agents externalize history [ 18 , 47 ], accumulating them in isolated sandboxes limits mu-tual connection. Our system addresses this limitation by connecting the agent’s independent social space directly to an active conversa-tional interface. This enables a dual-track memory mechanism: the agent simultaneously accumulates independent experiences in its virtual world and builds shared conversational memories with the user. By merging autonomous trajectories with user interactions, we shift the agent from a functional tool to an independent entity, establishing true relational equality. 

## 3 System Design 

Building upon the concept of generative agent sandboxes [ 26 ], we developed the Observable Social Life Spaces system to endow agents with continuous, independent virtual lives. The system en-ables agents to autonomously live, navigate, and socialize within a persistent virtual society while simultaneously supporting natu-ral language conversations with users. Crucially, it manages two distinct memory streams: experiences gathered from the agent’s independent virtual life, and shared conversational history with the user. By dynamically fusing these streams during dialogue, agent responses reflect both an autonomous life trajectory and a shared relational context (Figure 2). 

## 3.1 Design Principles 

Drawing on sociological theories of relationship formation, we op-erationalized four "social factor equality” principles to structure the agent’s virtual life: (a) Social Identity Equality. Based on Social Identity Theory [37 ] and Role Identity Theory [ 34 ], each agent is assigned a virtual professional identity corresponding to a real-world occupation (e.g., chef, musician, librarian). This identity is encoded in the agent’s profile and incorporated into the system prompt, influencing all dialogue and behavioral de-cisions. In this study, the primary agent is set as a restaurant chef to control experimental variables. (b) Social Relation Equality. Interpersonal relationships de-pend on mutual identity recognition [ 36 ]. The system de-ploys five agents with different personalities and occupa-tions within the Observable Social Life Spaces. Agents au-tomatically initiate multi-turn dialogues when spatial prox-imity falls below a threshold, forming a dynamic social network. Dialogue content among agents is recorded as virtual memory and naturally referenced in subsequent interactions with users. Would You Like to Visit My World? ## 3.2 User Interaction 

Users engage with the system through a touch-enabled interface, which allows them to freely toggle the visual observability of the agent’s virtual life. 

Unobservable Mode. In this mode, the interface displays only agent expressions. Users interact with agents through speech, which is transcribed into text and fed into the LLM along with the agent’s social identity and dialogue context (including dual-track long-term memory and recent short-term events) to generate responses. Responses are converted to speech via TTS. 

Observable Mode. Interaction processing and response gener-ation are identical to Unobservable mode. The difference is that users can observe agents’ activities within their Social Life Spaces, including: Agents’ movement trajectories across scenes (at walking pace, not teleportation); Ongoing activities upon arriving at a scene; Real-time dialogue between agents. 

## 3.3 System Overview 

The system architecture integrates three functional dimensions: autonomous virtual socialization, real-time user–agent dialogue, and dual-track memory integration. Agent behavior planning and memory summarization are driven by DeepSeek Chat, while real-time conversational responses are generated using GPT-4.1-mini. The continuous 2D environment maintains all agent states (lo-cation, current activity, memory, social history) in structured text, updating through a cyclic pipeline: (a) Behavior Planning. Agents are submitted to the LLM, which selects the next destination and activity intention based on role settings and current memory. (b) Path Execution. Planned actions are converted into step-wise movement sequences via A* path planning; agents move incrementally and enter activity states upon reaching destinations. (c) Social Trigger. The system detects spatially proximate agents and automatically initiates multi-turn dialogues when proximity criteria are met. Dialogue content is gener-ated iteratively by the LLM, which also controls conversa-tion termination. (d) Memory Update. Each movement, arrival, activity, and dialogue is recorded as a structured short-term memory event, which is compressed into long-term memory by the LLM once a threshold is reached. (e) Rendering. The environment is visualized from the target agent’s perspective, showing agent positions, activity labels, and dialogue bubbles in real-time. Users can speak to agents within this visualization. Agent re-sponses are generated by the LLM using role settings, dual-track memory, and dialogue history. If the agent accepts user-suggested actions (e.g., moving to a location or performing an activity), behav-ior planning is triggered immediately, allowing the user’s influence to be visible in real time. 

## 3.4 Memory Mechanism 

To support the agent’s continuous existence, we implemented a 

Dual-Track Memory Mechanism . This structure enables agents to parallelly process and integrate two distinct memory sources, each comprising short-term events and long-term summaries: 

Interaction Track. In Unobservable mode, each short-term mem-ory captures a single user–agent interaction, including what the user said and how agent responded. 

Social Life Space Track. Memories from agent activities in the Observable Social Life Spaces. Each short-term memory records a behavior or social event, e.g., "Anty arrived at the restaurant” or "Anty had a conversation with Barr: [dialogue content].” Short-term memories are structured event lists, while long-term memories are highly abstracted summaries generated by the LLM after accumulating a threshold of short-term events. During user interaction or behavioral decision-making, long-term memories and current short-term memories from both tracks are injected into the LLM prompt. This separate collection, separate storage, unified injection strategy allows agents to smoothly reference experiences independent of the user. For example, when asked about recent activities, an agent may report, "Today I met Barr in the garden and we talked about music.” Memories from the Observable Social Life Spaces thus endow the agent with a life trajectory extending beyond its service to the human. Anchored in the duality of human autobiographical memory [ 28 ], this mechanism concurrently main-tains both a shared interpersonal history (Interaction Track) and an independent reality (Social Life Space Track). Transitioning an agent from an appendage driven solely by user commands to an entity with parallel, independent trajectories forms the structural prerequisite for perceived equality. Methodologically, this continu-ous dual-track generation serves as a strictly controlled baseline. As detailed in Section 4, it allows us to isolate visual observability as the key experimental variable, ensuring that perceptual shifts arise strictly from interface-level affordances rather than confounding variations in generative capacity. 

## 4 User Study 4.1 Study Design 

We conducted a between-subjects study with 𝑁 = 24 participants (12 male, 12 female; ages 18–30; average age 24) recruited online. Participants interacted with the agent through a dedicated hard-ware interface: a flat, approximately circular touchscreen display terminal (roughly 5 cm in diameter). The protocol was approved by the Institutional Review Board; all participants provided informed consent and received USD 10 cash. To encourage natural conver-sation and minimize researcher intervention, the agent adopted a restaurant-chef persona. Would You Like to Visit My World? The study comprised three conditions (n=8 each): (1) Baseline (Interaction Only) : the agent relied exclusively on memories formed through direct user interaction (the conventional service-oriented paradigm); (2) Unobservable Life Space : the agent used the exact same dual-track memory architecture on the backend, generating an autonomous social life and incorporating it into dialogue, but this continuous life space remained visually inaccessible to the participant; (3) Observable Life Space : identical to the Unobservable condition in backend memory generation, but participants were invited to visually "visit" the continuous virtual space to observe the agent’s daily activities and autonomous interactions alongside direct dialogue. By running identical dual-track memory generation on the back-end across both the Unobservable and Observable conditions, we strictly held the agent’s generative capacity and accumulated "lived experiences" constant. Consequently, it allows us to test whether merely instilling a structural memory of independence is enough to alter relationship dynamics, or if visual observability is the re-quired threshold to translate this invisible memory into a tangible perception of equality. Each participant completed two sessions on separate days (around 15 mins each). Session 1 included a briefing, 5–10 min of free inter-action (audio/video recorded), and a 7-point Likert questionnaire adapted from the HALIE framework [ 23 ], with an additional di-mension integrated to measure perceived equality. Session 2 in-volved another free interaction, the same questionnaire, and a semi-structured interview. 

## 4.2 Measures 

We employed a mixed-methods approach, combining quantitative and qualitative analyses of the experimental data. For the quantitative measures, we treated Group, Gender, and Turn as the main independent variables and compared participants’ ratings across multiple subjective evaluation dimensions. We then conducted a mixed-design analysis of variance (ANOVA) to examine the effects of different experimental conditions on user perceptions [ 32 ], and further applied independent-sample t-tests [ 21 ] on key dimensions to confirm the significance of group differences. For the interview data, we applied thematic analysis following Braun and Clarke [ 6 ]. Transcripts were produced and anonymized prior to analysis. The first and second authors independently per-formed open coding on the full corpus, then met to reconcile differ-ences and synthesize a shared codebook. The second author audited the integrated coding for consistency across transcripts. Finally, the remaining authors reviewed the themes and provided critical feedback; discrepancies were resolved through discussion until con-sensus. This multi-analyst procedure (independent coding, audit, and team review) served as investigator triangulation to enhance the credibility and dependability of the findings. 

## 5 Results 5.1 Quantitative Results 

Significant trends emerged across both gender and round dimen-sions. As shown in Table 1, female participants consistently reported higher overall ratings than their male counterparts. Moreover, par-ticipants’ evaluations improved from the first to the second round (Table 2), indicating that repeated interactions foster more positive user perceptions and suggest that relational familiarity may play a role in shaping acceptance over time. Among the indicators, Equality showed a significant main effect of condition, F(2, N) = 4.62, p = 0.015. To further examine the source of this difference, independent-samples t-tests were conducted [ 21 ]. The results revealed that the Observable condition scored signifi-cantly higher than both the Baseline (t = -2.93, p = 0.006) and the Unobservable condition (t = -2.26, p = 0.031), while no significant difference was found between the Baseline and Unobservable con-ditions. This indicates that merely endowing the agent with an independent social life space did not suffice to alter participants’ perception of conversational equality; rather, it is only when this life space is visually observable that it significantly enhances their sense of interacting with an agent on an equal footing. 

## 5.2 Qualitative Results 

As summarized in Table 3, the proportion of participants who or-ganically described the agent using equal relational framing, such as referring to it as a peer, partner, or friend, increased when the independent social life space was introduced, and was highest when this space was visually observable. In the Baseline group, only 2/8 participants initially framed the agent as a partner or friend, rising to 4/8 after being told about its background world. In the Unob-servable group, this increased from 5/8 to 6/8. In the Observable group, 6/8 participants already perceived the agent as a partner or friend without further explanation. These shifts in relational framing strongly substantiate a higher degree of perceived equality. Meanwhile, participants who did not frame the agent as a partner or friend tended to describe it through a hierarchical, strictly tool-like, or functional lens (e.g., assistant, chef, service staff, stranger, pet). Through thematic analysis of the interview transcripts, we iden-tified several key findings.

This perception provided the agent with an identity beyond that of a subservient tool. 5.2.3 Other Factors and Their Impact on Equality. Users’ prior knowledge strongly shaped their relational expectations with the agent. Those with instrumental needs emphasized efficiency and retained a clear functional hierarchy (P24), while those with rela-tional needs interpreted the agent’s independent experiences as signs of authenticity, fostering more peer-like, equal relationships: "With this agent, I felt the relationship was more like talking with a friend.” (P3). Importantly, these orientations were fluid and some-times shifted with performance. Differences also emerged in how users perceived the authenticity of the agent’s role. Some participants quickly categorized the agent into preexisting cognitive schemas, and this categorization largely determined the nature of the relationship. Even when showing cu-riosity about the life world, some participants still viewed the agent as a "virtual child” (P17) or "electronic pet” (P24) rather than as an entity with independent thought. Users with richer prior knowl-edge of AI often dismissed the authenticity of the agent’s identity, attributing its responses to prewritten scripts or instrumental logic. As P2 put it: "It may say it has a life and a world . . . but I think that’s just a written setting. I don’t believe it really lives.” 

Overall, users sought anthropomorphism rather than full human-ization. They valued memory, empathy, and motivation but still ac-knowledged the agent’s artificial nature. This rational boundary al-lowed equal, human-like interactions yet enabled quick withdrawal when failures occurred: "Unless it really messes things up—like when DeepSeek wrote code that didn’t run and completely misunderstood my intent—I tend to interact with it on an equal basis, even giving it encouraging prompts. Still, when it fails badly, I immediately switch to ’boss mode.’” (p10) 

## 6 Discussion 6.1 Key Findings and Implications 

Our quantitative findings demonstrate that visual observability acts as the necessary catalyst for shifting relational dynamics. While users in the Unobservable condition acknowledged the agent’s background experiences, this cognitive awareness alone did not significantly alter the established baseline hierarchy. Presenting the agent’s life space visually provided the concrete grounding required for users to view the agent as a distinct social entity, which subsequently disrupted the standard command-execution loop. Qualitative findings echo these results. The visual observabil-ity of the agent’s independent social life substantially increased participants’ propensity to use equal relational framing. After the agent’s independent experiences were introduced, more partici-pants described the Agent as a peer, partner, or friend, with the strongest effect in the Observable condition, where 6/8 already adopted this relational framing without further explanation. In contrast, participants in the Baseline and Unobservable conditions often shifted from viewing the Agent as a subordinate tool only after being prompted about its background life. Other influencing factors include user demand orientation and role cognition. Users with instrumental needs treat the Agent as an exploratory object, whereas those with emotional needs more readily perceive its "authenticity.” Moreover, role framing (assistant, pet, etc.) shapes the relationship nature. Most participants preferred "human-like but not fully human,” valuing memory and empathy while recognizing the Agent’s artificiality. Sociological theories of long-term relationships emphasize equal positioning, non-instrumental interaction, and continuous mutual investment [ 10 , 15 , 38 ]. The Observable Social Life Spaces para-digm operationalizes these criteria within system design. By main-taining a dual-track memory and rendering the agent’s independent routine, the system provides users with evidence of an autonomous existence. This design trajectory encourages developers to prioritize agent independence alongside functional utility. 

## 6.2 Limitations and Future Directions 

Our work lays a foundation for perceived equality but has limita-tions that suggest future research directions. 

Subjective Measurement of Perceived Equality. Our conclusions regarding perceived equality rely heavily on self-reported question-naire data and qualitative interviews. Thus, the notion of equality captured in our study primarily reflects users’ subjective feelings and evaluations, rather than being strictly aligned with an objective or standardized psychological metric of relational equality. Future research should consider developing more rigorous, validated scales for human-agent equality to complement subjective self-reports with objective behavioral indicators. 

Privacy and Data Security. Regarding privacy, users’ uncertainty about personal information usage often led to guarded attitudes, hindering the formation of deeper relational bonds. To address this, future research could explore decentralized, privacy-preserving He et al. 

architectures that employ local storage and end-to-end encryption, ensuring that users can safely share intimate thoughts without fear of exposure [11, 12]. 

Longitudinal and Ecological Validity. Our experimental design relied on short-term interactions where participants were not na-tively immersed in a long-term deployment. Consequently, prior explanation of the agent’s autonomous mechanisms was required (especially in the Unobservable condition), which might have artifi-cially influenced their judgments. To overcome this, future work requires large-scale longitudinal studies involving demographically diverse users to evaluate relationship sustainability naturally. Such studies should also incorporate objective physiological indicators (e.g., heart rate variability and EEG) to systematically complement subjective reports on emotional bonding. 

Proactive and Multidimensional Personas. The current behavioral design exhibited limited initiative, primarily responding passively to user inputs. Because interactions often felt one-way, user rat-ings on empathy were negatively impacted. Also, the selected chef persona carries subtle service-oriented undertones that may arti-ficially reinforce a transactional dynamic. Future research should implement peer-like identities and develop interaction triggers that allow agents to initiate context-appropriate conversations based on their local memory. 

Expanding to Social Ecosystems. Moving beyond dyadic user-agent setups presents another distinct challenge. Deploying this paradigm in multi-user and multi-agent virtual environments could allow agents to act as social nodes rather than private utilities. Investigating how users interpret an agent interacting with other humans would provide deeper insight into scalable human-agent network dynamics. 

## 7 Applications 

We present four scenarios showing how observable social life spaces integrate with different interfaces, as shown in Fig. 3. 

## 7.1 Ambient Interfaces for Workflow Collaborators 

While recent systems visualize multi-agent workflows as dynamic virtual software companies [ 27 , 40 ], these agents still become in-active the moment task execution concludes. Rendering the ob-servable life space as a permanent ambient interface provides a visual state for the agent during these idle periods. When no tasks are assigned, the interface could display the agent navigating its environment or interacting with other background routines. By dragging a file or error log into this application window, users prompt the agent to explicitly route from its background activity to the execution thread, making the transition between independent state and workflow active and transparent. 

## 7.2 Mobile Companions with Persistent State 

Most digital companions utilize text interfaces [ 1 , 29 ] or static avatar GUIs [ 3 , 14 ]. Integrating continuous spatial rendering directly into mobile views allows users to check the agent’s current location and activity context prior to initiating a chat. Rather than opening a blank chat interface, the application loads the agent’s ongoing spatial activity view. If multiple users grant permission, agents from different devices could navigate to shared server instances to interact, generating asynchronous event logs that users can review upon opening their respective devices. 

## 7.3 Augmenting Embodied Agents during Idle States 

Hardware limitations restrict embodied agents from continuous operation, forcing them into extended charging or idle states. A paired mobile application or built-in chassis screen can render the virtual life space during these physical downtime windows. While the hardware remains static, the embodied agent could continue iterating spatial and social logic within the graphical sandbox. This ensures the system could remain active and observable to users regardless of battery or mechanical constraints. 

## 7.4 Spatially Anchored XR Companions 

Extended reality removes the mechanical constraints associated with physical hardware. In virtual reality (VR), the Observable So-cial Life Spaces act as entirely virtual, immersive environments [30 ] where the agent operates independent of real-world physics, demonstrating unconstrained action flexibility. Alternatively, in augmented reality (AR), mapping the virtual grid allows users to anchor the agent’s life space within the physical world, transform-ing it into a shared environment [ 20 ]. This approach spatializes the agent’s continuous routine without the overhead of robotic embodiment. 

## 8 Conclusion 

Current intelligent agents remain constrained by an instrumental, command-execution dynamic. To counter this, we introduced the 

Observable Social Life Spaces paradigm. By bringing an agent’s underlying autonomous experiences into a visually accessible envi-ronment, the system provides concrete evidence of an independent life extending beyond direct user commands. Through a mixed-methods user study ( 𝑁 = 24 ), we found that this visual observability is the key factor that drives relational shifts, significantly increasing users’ perception of equality ( 𝑝 = 0.015 ). Compared to standard prompt-response experiences, participants showed a greater will-ingness to treat the observable agent as a relational partner rather than a mere subordinate tool. Although evaluated in short-term sessions, these findings demonstrate that visualizing an agent’s independent context helps decouple it from user-servitude, gradu-ally steering human-agent interaction from functional utilization toward more equal interactions.
