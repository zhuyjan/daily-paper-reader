## Abstract 

Modern e-commerce search is inherently multimodal: customers make purchase decisions by jointly considering product text and visual informations. However, most industrial retrieval and ranking systems primarily rely on textual information, underutilizing the rich visual signals available in product images. In this work, we study unified text-image fusion for two-tower retrieval models in the e-commerce domain. We demonstrate that domain-specific fine-tuning and two stage alignment between query with product text and image modalities are both crucial for effective multimodal retrieval. Building on these insights, we propose a noval modality fusion network to fuse image and text information and capture cross-modal complementary information. Experiments on large-scale e-commerce datasets validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

## 1 Introduction 

Large-scale e-commerce retrieval systems are typically optimized around textual relevance, encoding user queries and product de-scriptions into a shared embedding space to enable efficient nearest-neighbor search at scale. While this design is computationally at-tractive and widely adopted in industry [ 3 , 12 ], it fails to reflect the inherently multimodal nature of user decision-making in online shopping. In practice, as illustrated in Figure 1, users often rely on visual information—such as appearance, style, color, and fine-grained details conveyed by product images—to assess relevance and make interaction decisions, especially when textual descrip-tions are sparse, ambiguous, or insufficient. This mismatch between the multimodal evidence users consider and the unimodal signals retrieval models are trained on leads to suboptimal relevance mod-eling, causing text-only retrieval methods to struggle in visually driven categories where subtle visual distinctions dominate rel-evance judgments. These limitations inspire the development of effective multimodal retrieval approaches that incorporate image information into representation learning, which has been shown to be beneficial for product retrieval and ranking [7, 10, 13, 18]. Despite substantial progress in multimodal representation learn-ing, applying multimodal techniques to e-commerce retrieval poses domain-specific challenges. VL-CLIP [ 4] points out weak object-level alignment and ambiguous textual representations in product data; EI-CLIP [ 11 ] identifies domain-specific entities as confounders and introduces causal interventions; and FashionKLIP [ 15 ] lever-ages external knowledge graphs to address the lack of fine-grained

While these approaches im-prove multimodal understanding, many introduce additional archi-tectural complexity or computational overhead that makes large-scale nearest-neighbor retrieval challenging [ 6, 8]. This motivates us to build an efficient multimodal retriever that maintains scal-able two-tower search, supports large-scale indexing, and can be deployed even in CPU-based production infrastructure. In this work, we directly address the mismatch between user decision signals and model learning signals in e-commerce retrieval by studying unified text–image fusion for two-tower retrieval mod-els. To effectively transfer general multi-modal models to the e-commerce domain„ we first show that domain-specific fine-tuning is essential for aligning multi-modal representations with com-mercial relevance. Building on this foundation, we explicitly align user queries with both product text and product images, enabling the model to learn from the same visual cues that users rely on during relevance judgment. Under this aligned setting, we con-duct systematic ablation studies on fusion strategies and propose a light-weight yet effective mixture-of-modality-experts fusion ar-chitecture, where each text and image encoder act as an expert, combined with bilinear interaction to model fine-grained cross-modal relationships. Extensive experiments demonstrate consistent improvements in retrieval effectiveness and downstream ranking performance. This work makes the following contributions to large-scale e-commerce retrieval: 

• We systematically analyze the role of product image infor-mation in retrieval, showing that visual signals play an im-portant role in improving retrieval effectiveness, especially for visually driven product categories. 

• We propose a mixture-of-modality-experts fusion architec-ture with an interaction network that enables effective inte-gration of textual and visual representations for multimodal retrieval.

## 2.3 Self-Adversarial Negative Sampling 

To improve training efficiency and robustness, we adopt a self-adversarial negative sampling strategy within each mini-batch. Given a batch of 𝑁 query–product pairs, we compute the cosine similarity matrix between query and product embeddings, where diagonal entries correspond to positives and off-diagonal entries provide candidate negatives. Instead of sampling negatives uniformly at random, we adopt similarity-weighted negative sampling. For each query, we use the cosine similarity scores as sampling weights and select the top-𝐾 

most similar products from the off-diagonal entries, with 𝐾 = 3 in our experiments. This self-adversarial strategy exposes the model to highly confusable yet irrelevant items, improving discriminative representation learning and accelerating convergence compared to random sampling [5, 16, 17].

## 2.5 Curriculum Training Strategy 

We adopt a curriculum-style multi-stage training strategy to pro-gressively align model representations with user decision signals in e-commerce search [ 5 , 9 ]. Instead of directly optimizing query–item relevance in a fully multimodal space, we decompose the learning process into three stages with increasing supervision complexity, Beyond Text: Aligning Vision and Language for Multimodal E-Commerce Retrieval Conference’17, July 2017, Washington, DC, USA

allowing the model to gradually acquire modality-aware and cross-modal relevance patterns. 

Stage I: Domain Adaptation of CLIP Encoders. In the first stage, we perform domain-specific fine-tuning of the CLIP encoders on a large-scale title–product dataset constructed from Target prod-uct information. Contrastive learning is applied between product titles and product images to adapt pretrained visual-text represen-tations to e-commerce semantics. 

Stage II: Modality-Specific Query Alignment. In the second stage, we explicitly align user queries with individual item modali-ties. Queries are separately aligned with product titles and product images using the proposed three-part hinge loss. This curriculum step encourages the model to learn modality-specific relevance signals while incorporating graded ranking supervision. 

Stage III: Multimodal Fusion Alignment. In the final stage, we align queries with the fused product representations produced by the proposed mixture-of-modality-experts architecture. This stage enables the model to learn unified multimodal embeddings that capture both unimodal evidence and cross-modal interactions, resulting in more effective retrieval and improved downstream ranking performance. 

## 3 Experiments 3.1 Training Dataset 

We construct our training data from three months of e-commerce search logs, yielding the top 20M aggregated query–item pairs. Each item is represented by its product title and image. To capture user preference, we derive a desirability label from aggregated interaction signals such as clicks, add-to-cart, and pur-chases. In addition, we incorporate an auxiliary semantic relevance signal by applying a cross-encoder trained on human-annotated relevance data to estimate query–item matching scores over the corpus. These complementary supervision signals jointly guide model training. 

## 3.2 Evaluation Dataset 

We evaluate our approach on two held-out benchmarks capturing complementary aspects of retrieval quality: user preference align-ment ( desirability ) and semantic relevance ( relevance ). To prevent leakage, all evaluation queries are removed from the training set. 

Desirability. A log-based dataset from a disjoint time window reflecting graded user engagement. It contains 248,593 query–item pairs over 6,284 queries, with 40 relevant items per query on aver-age. 

Relevance. A human-annotated dataset providing gold-standard semantic judgments. It includes 400,515 pairs from 4,893 queries, with an average of 82 items per query. 

## 3.3 Experimental Setup 

We evaluate a series of two-tower retrieval variants to quantify the contribution of visual signals, alignment strategies, and fusion architectures. All multimodal models are built upon CLIP-based text and image encoders, differing only in their adaptation and fusion designs: 

• Text-only (Baseline). A standard two-tower retriever using only the CLIP text encoder over queries and product titles. 

• Pretrained CLIP (MoE Fusion). A multimodal retriever that incorporates product images using pretrained CLIP encoders with a modality mixture-of-experts fusion module. 

• Domain Fine-Tuning. The CLIP encoders are first adapted to the e-commerce domain via contrastive training on 4M title– image pairs before multimodal retrieval. 

• Query Alignment. Beyond domain adaptation, queries are explicitly aligned with product text and image modalities in a staged manner to better capture user relevance signals. 

• Fusion Ablations. We further compare alternative fusion de-signs, including MLP fusion, MoE+MLP fusion, attention-based fusion, and our proposed MoE+Bilinear interaction network. Conference’17, July 2017, Washington, DC, USA Qujiaheng Zhang, Guangyue Xu, and Fengjie Li

## 3.5 Experimental Results 

We first evaluate the overall impact of incorporating product im-age information into two-tower retrieval models. Tables 1 compare the text-only baseline with the proposed text+image model using MoE with bilinear fusion. The multimodal model yields consistent and substantial improvements across all NDCG cutoffs for both desirability and relevance objectives. In particular, we observe up to 4.86% improvement in NDCG@1 for desirability and 2.36% im-provement for relevance, demonstrating that visual signals provide complementary information beyond textual representations and significantly enhance retrieval quality. 

## 3.6 Ablation Studies 

3.6.1 Ablation on Domain Fine-Tuning and Query Alignment. We further study the effect of different multimodal alignment strate-gies. Tables 2 compare three variants: using pretrained CLIP en-coders, domain fine-tuning CLIP on large-scale product–image pairs, and further aligning queries with both product titles and images. Domain-specific fine-tuning consistently improves both desirability and relevance metrics over pretrained CLIP, indicating better adaptation to e-commerce semantics. Moreover, explicitly aligning queries with text and image modalities yields strong addi-tional gains across all cutoffs, highlighting the importance of second stage alignment to reduce the mismatch between user intent and item representations. 

3.6.2 Ablation on Fusion Networks. Table 3 compares five multi-modal fusion architectures. The proposed MoE + bilinear fusion consistently achieves the strongest performance on both desirability and relevance evaluations. The results indicate that adaptive modal-ity weighting alone is insufficient to capture complex text–image relationships. By explicitly modeling fine-grained cross-modal in-teractions, bilinear fusion enables the model to better exploit com-plementary visual and textual cues, particularly in visually driven retrieval scenarios. Overall, these findings suggest that effective multimodal retrieval benefits from jointly modeling modality im-portance and expressive cross-modal interactions.

## 4 Conclusion 

In this work, we studied unified text–image fusion for two-tower re-trieval models in the e-commerce domain. We showed that incorpo-rating product images significantly improves both desirability and relevance ranking, and that domain-specific fine-tuning together with explicit query alignment to text and image modalities is cru-cial for effective multimodal retrieval. Our proposed a mixture-of-modality-experts architecture with bilinear interaction demonstrate consistent gains across multiple evaluation metrics with analysis further revealing that the model learns adaptive modality weighting and meaningful cross-modal interactions. These findings highlight the importance of aligning multi-modal model learning with user decision signals and provide practical guidance for building scalable multi-modal retrieval systems. Future work includes extending the framework to support image-based search by incorporating visual encoders into the query tower, enabling unified text and image queries within the same retrieval framework.
