Abstract 

On-policy distillation (OPD) improves reasoning mod-els by applying dense teacher supervision on student-sampled trajectories. However, scaling OPD to long-horizon mathematical reasoning exposes a reliability and efficiency problem: standard OPD assigns every sampled candidate the same long rollout budget, even though some trajectories may quickly become weakly aligned with the teacher and provide less useful super-vision. Prior analyses suggest that successful OPD de-pends on local teacher–student compatibility, which can be measured by top-k overlap on student-visited pre-fixes. When this overlap is low, continuing to generate or train on long suffixes may waste computation and in-troduce noisy learning signal. To address this, we intro-duce Prefix-Guided On-Policy Distillation (PG-OPD), a simple rollout-allocation framework that uses fixed-length prefixes to estimate trajectory value before ex-pensive long-horizon generation. PG-OPD first decodes every sampled candidate to the same prefix length, computes teacher–student top-k overlap within an early probe window of that prefix, and selectively continues high-overlap candidates to a fixed long length. Low-overlap candidates stop at the fixed prefix, avoiding unnecessary suffix generation. Across diverse teacher– student combinations on AMC, AIME, and HMMT benchmarks, PG-OPD improves average accuracy by up to 4.80 points while reducing training time by up to 2.46 ×. These results suggest that prefix-level compati-bility provides a practical signal for directing OPD com-putation toward trajectories that remain learnable from the teacher. 

## Introduction 

On-policy distillation (OPD) has become an increasingly important post-training paradigm for language-model rea-soning. Classical knowledge distillation transfers the behav-ior of a strong teacher to a smaller student by matching soft output distributions or teacher-generated sequences (Hinton, Vinyals, and Dean, 2015; Kim and Rush, 2016; Sanh et al., 2019; Jiao et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020). However, off-policy distillation trains the student on fixed teacher tra-jectories, which can introduce exposure bias: at inference time, the student must condition on prefixes produced by its own policy rather than by the teacher (Bengio et al.,

Prefix-guided selection identifies high-value trajectories from early prefixes and al-locates long-rollout budget to them, improving both avg@16 accuracy and training efficiency. 2015). OPD addresses this mismatch by sampling trajec-tories from the current student and applying teacher super-vision on the same student-visited states (Gu et al., 2024; Agarwal et al., 2024). This makes OPD especially attrac-tive for long-horizon mathematical reasoning, where dense token-level supervision can provide richer learning signals than sparse final-answer rewards (Lu and Lab, 2025; Yang et al., 2025; Guo et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025). Despite its effectiveness, OPD is computationally expen-sive. A typical OPD update samples multiple candidate tra-jectories for every prompt and decodes each candidate to a long response budget. For mathematical reasoning, where solutions can span thousands of tokens, rollout generation becomes one of the dominant costs of training. Existing rea-soning post-training pipelines often rely on large-scale roll-outs to obtain exploration and dense supervision (Shao et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025; He et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025). In OPD, this cost is further amplified because teacher feedback is evaluated on student-generated prefixes. As a result, full OPD spends comparable computation on all sampled trajec-tories, even though different trajectories may have very dif-

Recent analyses suggest that the success of OPD depends not only on teacher strength, but also on local compatibil-ity between the teacher and student on student-visited pre-fixes (Li et al., 2026; Fu et al., 2026; Jang et al., 2026; Jin et al., 2026). A useful diagnostic is teacher–student top-

k overlap: when the student and teacher assign probabil-ity mass to similar candidate tokens, the teacher is more likely to provide locally exploitable supervision. Conversely, low-overlap prefixes may indicate that the student’s trajec-tory has drifted away from the teacher’s reasoning process. Training on such low-compatibility regions can waste roll-out computation and may even introduce noisy or mislead-ing dense supervision. This issue is particularly important in long-horizon reasoning, because even trajectories that begin with high overlap can gradually diverge as generation pro-ceeds. This motivates a different view of efficient OPD: the central question is not only how to shorten rollouts, but how to allocate rollout budget according to prefix-level dis-tillation value. Uniformly decoding every sampled trajec-tory to the same maximum length assumes that all candi-dates are equally worth continuing, even though their early prefixes may already exhibit very different teacher–student compatibility. Some prefixes remain close to the teacher’s high-probability token region and are likely to receive lo-cally exploitable dense supervision if continued, while oth-ers quickly move into low-overlap regions where additional decoding mainly produces long suffixes whose teacher sig-nal is weak, noisy, or difficult for the student to learn from. Treating these two types of trajectories identically wastes computation and may dilute the useful on-policy signal in the training batch. A fixed prefix therefore provides a natural opportunity to decide whether a candidate deserves expen-sive long-horizon generation before the full response is pro-duced. By evaluating teacher–student overlap on this prefix, OPD can obtain a lightweight estimate of whether the trajec-tory remains aligned with the teacher’s local action space. What OPD lacks is a simple rollout-allocation mechanism: instead of assigning the same response budget to every sam-pled candidate, it should use prefix-level compatibility to de-cide which trajectories deserve long continuations. We propose Prefix-Guided On-Policy Distillation (PG-OPD), a rollout-allocation framework for efficient OPD. PG-OPD first generates a short prefix for each sampled candi-date, computes teacher–student top-k overlap on the prefix, and then selectively allocates long continuations to trajecto-ries with higher prefix-level compatibility. In this way, PG-OPD avoids blindly spending the same long rollout budget on every candidate and instead directs computation toward trajectories that are more likely to provide useful distillation signal. The method is simple, model-agnostic, and can be implemented as a lightweight modification to standard OPD. We evaluate PG-OPD across diverse teacher–student combinations on challenging mathematical reasoning benchmarks, including AMC, AIME, and HMMT. Across these settings, PG-OPD consistently reduces training time while preserving, and often improving, downstream accu-racy. These results suggest that long-horizon OPD should not be governed by a uniform rollout budget alone. Instead, prefix-level compatibility provides a practical signal for reallocating computation toward trajectories that are more learnable for the student. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • We identify rollout allocation as a key efficiency bottle-neck in long-horizon OPD and propose using fixed-length prefix compatibility to estimate trajectory value. • We introduce PG-OPD, a simple prefix-guided rollout al-location method that selectively continues high-value tra-jectories while keeping the standard OPD objective un-changed. • We validate PG-OPD across multiple teacher–student combinations and mathematical reasoning benchmarks, showing consistent average-accuracy improvements and substantial wall-clock speedups. • We provide ablations on prefix length, random pruning, prompt-minimum allocation, and prefix-based ranking to analyze why PG-OPD works. 

## Related Work 

On-Policy Distillation 

Knowledge distillation transfers knowledge from a teacher model to a student model through distribution matching or teacher-generated trajectories (Hinton, Vinyals, and Dean, 2015; Kim and Rush, 2016). Recent work extends this paradigm to on-policy distillation (OPD), where supervision is computed on trajectories sampled from the current student policy rather than on fixed teacher-generated data (Gu et al., 2024; Agarwal et al., 2024). By evaluating teacher distribu-tions on student-visited states, OPD reduces train–inference mismatch and provides dense token-level learning signals throughout generation. This formulation has become an im-portant component of modern post-training pipelines for rea-soning models (Lu and Lab, 2025; Yang et al., 2025; Guo et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025). OPD has also been extended to self-distillation and online learning settings, where models improve through their own generated trajectories and feed-back signals (H¨ ubotter and others, 2026; Shenfeld and oth-ers, 2026; Zhao and others, 2026). Our work focuses on im-proving the efficiency of OPD while preserving its on-policy learning advantages. 

Trajectory Value in OPD 

Recent studies have begun to investigate why OPD succeeds or fails under different teacher–student configurations. Li et al. (2026) show that successful OPD depends on thinking-pattern consistency between the student and teacher, and that benchmark performance alone does not determine distilla-tion effectiveness. They further identify overlap ratio be-tween teacher and student top-k distributions as a key in-dicator of successful distillation, demonstrating that high-probability overlap tokens contribute most of the optimiza-tion signal. Subsequent work reports similar observations regarding teacher–student compatibility, reward quality, and learning dynamics in OPD (Fu et al., 2026; Jang et al., 2026; Jin et al., 2026). These analyses suggest that different trajec-tories contribute unequally to distillation. In particular, tra-jectories with high teacher–student overlap are more likely to provide useful learning signals, whereas low-overlap tra-jectories may offer limited benefit. Moreover, the value of a trajectory can change throughout generation, as initially aligned prefixes may gradually drift away from the teacher’s reasoning process. Our work builds on these observations and studies how trajectory value can be used to allocate roll-out computation more effectively. 

Efficient OPD 

As OPD becomes increasingly important for long-horizon reasoning, several works have explored reducing its compu-tational cost. Recent approaches improve efficiency through modified objectives, adaptive targets, or alternative super-vision strategies (Ko et al., 2026; Jang et al., 2026; Jin et al., 2026). Most closely related to our work, PRUNE-OPD dynamically truncates rollouts when teacher supervision be-comes unreliable under prefix drift, reducing computation spent on low-quality suffixes (Yang et al., 2026). While these methods primarily focus on improving supervision quality or controlling rollout length during training, our approach addresses a complementary problem: how to allocate rollout budgets across sampled trajectories before expensive long continuations are generated. PG-OPD uses short prefixes as a lightweight estimate of trajectory value and selectively al-locates continuation budgets according to teacher–student overlap, thereby reducing rollout cost while preserving use-ful distillation signal.

## Experiments 

Setup 

We evaluate PG-OPD on five mathematical reasoning benchmarks: AIME24, AIME25, AMC23, HMMT24, and HMMT25. The main setting uses DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B as the student and JustRL-DeepSeek-1.5B as the teacher. To evaluate whether the proposed rollout-allocation strategy generalizes across different model fam-ilies and teacher–student relationships, we further test three additional settings: OpenMath-1.5B distilled from JustRL-Nemotron-1.5B, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B distilled from DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B, and Qwen3-4B-Base distilled from Qwen3-4B. All methods are trained on the same DAPO-Math-17K data under the same optimizer, sampling configuration, and number of optimization steps. Unless otherwise specified, we train for 1 epoch with a training mini-batch size of 64 and sample 4 responses per prompt during training. Roll-outs use temperature 1.0, teacher temperature 1.0, repetition penalty 1.0, and top-k = 16 log-probability extraction. We use the student-probability weighting variant for token-level distillation rewards and disable KL regularization and for-mat rewards. The maximum prompt length is 1024 tokens. For 1.5B and Qwen3 experiments, the maximum training response length is 7168 tokens unless a method explicitly changes the effective rollout budget. Unless otherwise specified, PG-OPD uses a prefix length of P = 5000 and an overlap-scoring window of R = 128 

probe tokens. We evaluate pruning ratios of 25%, 50%, and 75%, where the pruning ratio denotes the fraction of sampled candidates that are not continued after the prefix-selection stage. Prefix tokens from all candidates are re-tained for training, while only selected candidates are de-coded beyond the prefix stage. For evaluation, we sample 16 solutions per problem with temperature 0.7, top-p = 0 .95 , and a maximum generation length of 31744 tokens. We report mean rollout accuracy over the 16 sampled solutions. Training time is measured as wall-clock time in seconds under the same training setup for each teacher–student pair. Speedup is computed relative to the OPD baseline under the same teacher–student setting. 

Compared Methods 

OPD. The baseline follows standard on-policy distillation. It samples trajectories from the current student policy, de-codes every sampled trajectory to the full response budget, and trains on all generated tokens. 

PRUNE-OPD. PRUNE-OPD (Yang et al., 2026) monitors teacher–student top-k overlap during generation and trun-cates a rollout once the overlap falls below a threshold. It re-duces OPD cost through online trajectory truncation, rather than prefix-stage candidate selection. 

PG-OPD. Prefix-Guided OPD first generates a short pre-fix for each sampled candidate, uses teacher–student top-k

overlap on the prefix to estimate trajectory value, and then allocates long continuations only to selected candidates. Un-less otherwise specified, all candidates contribute their pre-fix tokens to training, while only selected candidates are de-coded beyond the prefix stage.

## Conclusion 

Existing OPD methods provide dense teacher supervision on student-sampled trajectories, but they usually assign the same long rollout budget to every sampled candidate. This uniform strategy becomes inefficient in long-horizon rea-soning: rollout generation dominates training cost, and tra-jectories with low teacher–student compatibility may pro-vide weak or even harmful distillation signal, especially as student-generated prefixes drift away from the teacher’s reasoning process. Motivated by these limitations, Prefix-Guided On-Policy Distillation (PG-OPD) uses short prefixes to estimate trajectory value via teacher–student top-k over-lap and selectively allocates long continuations to promis-ing candidates. This design keeps the standard OPD ob-jective unchanged while reducing unnecessary long-horizon generation. Across multiple teacher–student combinations and five mathematical reasoning benchmarks, PG-OPD im-proves the compute–performance tradeoff of OPD, achiev-ing up to 4.80 points of average-score improvement and up to 2.46 × wall-clock speedup across all settings. Ablation studies further show that prefix length, prefix-guided selec-tion, and prompt-level continuation coverage all contribute to stable performance under aggressive pruning. These re-sults suggest that efficient OPD should not blindly decode every sampled trajectory to full length; instead, prefix-level compatibility provides a practical signal for reallocating computation toward trajectories that are more useful for dis-tillation. 

## Limitations 

PG-OPD relies on prefix-level top-k overlap as a proxy for trajectory value. Although the ranking analysis shows that this signal is useful in our settings, it may be less reliable when teacher and student use substantially different reason-ing styles or tokenization behaviors. The method also intro-duces hyperparameters such as prefix length and continu-ation budget, whose optimal values may vary across mod-els and tasks. Finally, our experiments focus on mathemati-cal reasoning benchmarks; extending PG-OPD to multi-turn, tool-use, or agentic settings remains future work.
